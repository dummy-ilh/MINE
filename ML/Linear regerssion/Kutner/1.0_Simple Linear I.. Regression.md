## Overview

**Regression analysis** is a statistical methodology that helps us understand and predict relationships between variables. Think of it as a mathematical way to answer questions like:
- "If I increase my advertising budget, how much will sales increase?"
- "How does a child's vocabulary relate to their age and parents' education?"
Or
**Regression analysis** is a powerful statistical technique used to model the relationship between a **dependent variable** (also called response variable, outcome variable) and one or more **independent variables** (also called predictor variables, explanatory variables, or covariates). The primary goals of regression analysis are:
1.  **Prediction:** To predict the value of the dependent variable based on the values of the independent variables.
2.  **Inference:** To understand the nature and strength of the relationship between the variables, and to assess the effect of independent variables on the dependent variable.
3.  **Modeling:** To construct a mathematical equation that describes the relationship.
   
This chapter introduces **simple linear regression** . "Simple" means we're using just one predictor variable to predict an outcome.

## 1.1 Relations Between Variables

### Functional vs. Statistical Relations

There are two fundamental types of relationships between variables:

#### 1. **Functional Relation** (Perfect/Deterministic)

A functional relation is expressed by an exact mathematical formula. Every input produces exactly one predictable output. In this relationship, one variable can be perfectly predicted from another (e.g., $F = 1.8C + 32$). There's no random error

**Mathematical form**: If $X$ is the **independent variable** and $Y$ is the **dependent variable**, then:

$$Y = f(X)$$

**📊 Example 1.1: Dollar Sales**

If a product sells for $2 per unit, the relationship between units sold ($X$) and dollar sales ($Y$) is:

$$Y = 2X$$

| Period | Units Sold (X) | Dollar Sales (Y) |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| 1 | 75 | $150 |
| 2 | 25 | $50 |
| 3 | 130 | $260 |

Notice that all points fall *exactly* on the line $Y = 2X$. There's no variation - this is characteristic of functional relations.


**Key characteristics of functional relations:**
- Perfect predictability
- No scatter around the relationship curve
- Common in physical sciences and accounting

#### 2. **Statistical Relation** (Imperfect/Probabilistic)

A statistical relation is *not* perfect. The observations scatter around a general tendency or curve of relationship. While variables might tend to move together, the relationship isn't perfect. There's inherent variability, meaning that for a given independent variable, the dependent variable can take on a range of values due to unobserved factors, measurement errors, or inherent randomness. Regression analysis deals with statistical relationships, aiming to capture the systematic part while modeling random variation.

**📊 Example 1.2: Employee Performance Evaluation**

Data on 10 employees showing midyear performance evaluation ($X$) and year-end evaluation ($Y$):


**Key observations:**
- Higher midyear scores tend to associate with higher year-end scores (positive trend)
- The relationship is NOT perfect - there's **scatter** around the trend
- Two employees with midyear score of 80 had *different* year-end scores
- This scatter is typical of statistical relations

**Why the scatter?** Year-end performance isn't determined *solely* by midyear performance. Other factors affect it:
- Employee motivation changes
- External circumstances
- Measurement error in evaluations
- Random factors

> ⚠️ **Important Distinction**: 
> - **Functional relation**: Every X value gives exactly one Y value
> - **Statistical relation**: Each X value is associated with a *probability distribution* of Y values

**📊 Example 1.3: Age and Steroid Level**

Data from 27 healthy females (ages 8-25) showing plasma steroid levels:

![Curvilinear Statistical Relation](placeholder-curvilinear-relation.png)

**Key insights:**
- The relationship is **curvilinear** (not a straight line)
- Steroid levels increase with age up to a point, then begin to level off or decrease
- Substantial scatter around the curve
- This illustrates that statistical relations can have various shapes

> 💡 **Real-world insight**: Most real-world relationships are statistical, not functional. Biological, social, and business phenomena involve many interacting factors, creating inherent variability.

## 1.2 Regression Models and Their Uses

### Historical Origins

The term "regression" comes from **Sir Francis Galton** (19th century), who studied inheritance of physical traits:

**Galton's Discovery**: Children of very tall or very short parents tended to be closer to the average height than their parents. He called this tendency to **"regress" to the mean** or return toward **"mediocrity."**
He observed that while tall parents tended to have tall children, the children's heights, on average, "**regressed**" or "moved back" towards the average height of the population. This phenomenon is known as "regression to the mean." Although modern regression analysis is used for much more than this specific biological phenomenon, the name "regression" stuck. It now broadly refers to statistical methods that model relationships between variables, predicting one from others.


> 📚 **Historical note**: The term "regression" persists today even though we now use it much more broadly than Galton's original application.

### Basic Concepts of Regression Models

A **regression model** formalizes the two essential features of a statistical relation:

#### The Two Essential Ingredients

1. **A systematic tendency**: The response variable $Y$ varies with the predictor $X$ in some systematic fashion
2. **Scatter around the trend**: Points don't fall exactly on the curve due to variability

**How regression models capture this:**

1. **Probability distribution of Y for each X**: For any given level of $X$, there exists a probability distribution of possible $Y$ values

2. **Systematic relationship between means**: The means of these probability distributions vary systematically with $X$

**📊 Example (continued): Performance Evaluation**

For midyear evaluation $X = 90$, there's a probability distribution of possible year-end evaluations $Y$:

![Probability Distribution Illustration](placeholder-probability-distribution.png)

The **regression model** says:
- Each X level (50, 70, 90, etc.) has its own probability distribution of Y
- The *means* of these distributions follow a systematic pattern (the regression curve)
- Individual observations scatter around this curve due to randomness

### Regression Function

The **regression function** (also called regression curve) describes the systematic relationship between the means of the probability distributions and the level of $X$.

**Mathematical notation**:

$$E\{Y\} = f(X)$$

Where:
- $E\{Y\}$ = expected value (mean) of Y
- $f(X)$ = regression function
- "The mean of Y varies systematically with X"

**Visual interpretation**: The regression curve connects the means of all the probability distributions.

![Pictorial Representation of Regression Model](placeholder-regression-model-visual.png)

**Key points about the regression function:**
- It may be linear or curved
- It describes the *average* relationship
- Individual observations deviate from it by the error term
- It's the "signal" we're trying to detect amidst the "noise"

### Regression Models with Multiple Predictors

Real-world problems often involve multiple predictor variables:

**📊 Example: Branch Office Operating Costs**

**Response variable**: Direct operating cost for the year
**Predictor variables** (4 total):
- Average size of loan outstanding
- Average number of loans outstanding  
- Total number of new loan applications processed
- Index of office salaries

**📊 Example: Tractor Purchase Study**

**Response variable**: Volume of tractor purchases (in horsepower)
**Predictor variables** (9 total):

**📊 Example: Medical Study**

**Response variable**: Peak plasma growth hormone level
**Predictor variables** (14 total):
- Age, gender, height, weight

**Mathematical extension**: With two predictors $X_1$ and $X_2$, the regression model extends to:

$$E\{Y\} = f(X_1, X_2)$$

This becomes a **regression surface** rather than a curve. We'll cover this in later chapters.

### Construction of Regression Models

Building a good regression model involves several important decisions:

#### Decision 1: **Selection of Predictor Variables**

**Key principle**: Include only a *limited* number of predictor variables for any given situation.

**Why limit the number?**
- Reality must be simplified to manageable proportions
- Not all factors can or should be included
- Some factors may not be measurable

**How to choose?**
A predictor should be included if it contributes to:
1. **Reducing remaining variation** in Y after accounting for other predictors
2. **Practical considerations**: 
   - Can it be obtained accurately?
   - Is it economical to measure?
   - Will it improve predictions meaningfully?

**Example consideration**: Should we include "employee aptitude" in our model?
- Does it add predictive value beyond other variables?
- Can we measure aptitude reliably?
- Is the cost of testing worthwhile?

> 💡 **Practical wisdom**: Adding more predictors isn't always better. The goal is parsimony - the simplest model that captures the essential relationships.

#### Decision 2: **Functional Form of Regression Relation**

The regression function's shape is tied to which predictors we choose, but we must still decide on its mathematical form.

**Two main scenarios:**

**A. Theory guides the form**

Sometimes, relevant theory indicates the appropriate functional form.

**Example**: Learning theory suggests that unit production cost should have an asymptotic shape:
- Initially decreases rapidly as experience accumulates
- Eventually levels off as learning plateaus

This theoretical knowledge suggests using a specific functional form with particular asymptotic properties.

**B. Empirical approximation** (more common)

Often, we don't have strong theoretical guidance, so we:

1. Collect the data
2. Examine it empirically (plots, exploratory analysis)
3. Choose a functional form as a *first approximation*
4. Test whether it fits adequately

**Common first choices:**
- **Linear functions**: Simple and often adequate
- **Quadratic functions**: Capture simple curvature

**Why linear or quadratic?**

Even when we know the true relationship is complex, linear or quadratic functions often provide **good approximations** over the range of data we have.

![Linear Approximation to Complex Function](placeholder-linear-approximation.png)

**Figure 1.5** shows how:
- A simple linear function (dotted line) can approximate a complex true function (bold line)
- A piecewise linear function (two straight segments) can approximate even better

> ⚠️ **Critical caveat**: The chosen functional form is valid only within the **scope of the model** - the range of predictor values in your data.

**📊 Example: Price and Sales Volume**

A company studied sales volume at 6 price levels ranging from $4.95 to $6.95:

- Scope of model: $5 to $7
- Shape of regression function: We only know its shape in this range
- Below $4.95 or above $6.95: The investigation provides *no evidence* about the relationship

**Why?** Different mechanisms might operate outside the studied range:
- Below $4.95: Consumers might perceive the product as "cheap" and reduce purchases
- Above $6.95: The relationship might change dramatically

> 💡 **Fundamental principle**: Never extrapolate beyond the scope of your data without additional justification.

#### Decision 3: **Scope of the Model**

When formulating a regression model, we must define its coverage:
- What range of predictor values?
- What time period?
- What conditions or settings?

**The scope is determined by**:
1. The design of the investigation
2. The range of available data

**Typical scope determination process:**
1. Investigation design specifies ranges (e.g., price from $5-$7)
2. Data are collected within those ranges
3. Model is valid only for those ranges

### Uses of Regression Analysis

Regression serves **three major purposes**: description, control, and prediction. These often overlap in practice.

#### Purpose 1: **Description**

Develop a "usable" statistical relation that describes the relationship, even if we don't fully understand the underlying causal mechanisms.

**📊 Example: Tractor Purchase Study**

- **Goal**: Understand how tractor purchases relate to farm characteristics and territory conditions
- **Use**: Management can describe relationships and develop insights
- **Note**: Description doesn't require perfect understanding of causation

**Value**: Even without causal knowledge, describing the relationship helps:
- Administrative control and planning
- Understanding of the business environment
- Setting realistic expectations

#### Purpose 2: **Control** (Administrative Control)

Use the regression relation to set standards and monitor performance by:
1. Developing a usable statistical relation between cost and key predictor variables
2. Using this to set cost standards for each branch office
3. Comparing actual costs against expected costs
4. Investigating offices with large deviations

**📊 Example: Branch Office Operating Costs**

**Process:**
1. Build regression model: Expected cost = f(loan size, loan count, applications, salaries)
2. For each office, calculate expected cost based on its characteristics
3. Compare actual cost to expected cost
4. Flag offices where: Actual cost >> Expected cost

**Benefits:**
- Fair comparisons (accounts for different operating conditions)
- Identifies outlier offices needing attention
- Can predict costs for the fiscal year
- Enables proactive management

**Later applications:**
- Compare actual year-end cost against predicted cost
- This dual use (control + prediction) is common

#### Purpose 3: **Prediction**

Predict growth hormone deficiencies in short children using simple, inexpensive measurements rather than complex medical tests.

**📊 Example: Medical Study of Short Children**

**Traditional approach:** Expensive, invasive tests
**Regression approach:** 
- Use statistical relation to predict hormone levels
- Based on: age, gender, height, weight, skinfold measurements
- Much simpler and less expensive

**Value:** 
- Reduces medical costs
- Makes screening more accessible
- Still provides useful predictions for clinical decision-making

> 💡 **Important note**: These three purposes frequently overlap. The branch office example uses regression for both control (setting standards) and prediction (forecasting costs).

---


### Regression and Causality

It's crucial to understand that **correlation does not imply causation**. While regression analysis can identify and quantify relationships, it cannot, by itself, prove causality.

* **Association:** Regression shows a strong statistical association.
* **Causation:** To infer causation, one needs to consider:
    * **Temporal precedence:** X must precede Y.
    * **Plausible mechanism:** A logical reason for X to cause Y.
    * **Elimination of confounding variables:** Other variables influencing both X and Y must be controlled for.
    Controlled experiments are often needed to establish causality, whereas observational studies are less conclusive.

## 1.3 Simple Linear Regression Model with Distribution of Error Terms Unspecified

Now we get specific and introduce the most basic regression model: **simple linear regression**.

### Why "Simple" and "Linear"?

- **Simple**: Only one predictor variable
- **Linear in the parameters**: No parameter appears as an exponent or is multiplied/divided by another parameter
- **Linear in the predictor variable**: The predictor variable appears only in the first power (not $X^2$, $X^3$, etc.)

### Formal Statement of the Model

The simple linear regression model is:

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i \quad \text{(1.1)}$$

**Where:**

| Symbol | Name | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| $Y_i$ | Response value | The value of the response variable in the $i$-th trial/observation |
| $\beta_0$ | Intercept parameter | Y-intercept of the regression line (unknown constant) |
| $\beta_1$ | Slope parameter | Slope of the regression line (unknown constant) |
| $X_i$ | Predictor value | A known constant - the value of the predictor variable in the $i$-th trial |
| $\epsilon_i$ | Error term | Random error term with mean $E\{\epsilon_i\} = 0$ and variance $\sigma^2\{\epsilon_i\} = \sigma^2$ |
| $i$ | Index | $i = 1, 2, \ldots, n$ (where $n$ = number of observations) |

**Additional assumptions about error terms:**
- All $\epsilon_i$ are **uncorrelated**: $\sigma\{\epsilon_i, \epsilon_j\} = 0$ for all $i \neq j$
- Equivalently, their covariance is zero: $\text{Cov}(\epsilon_i, \epsilon_j) = 0$ for all $i \neq j$

### Terminology Note

Model (1.1) is called a **first-order model** because:
- The predictor variable $X$ appears only in the first power
- There are no higher powers like $X^2$ or $X^3$

It's also called:
- **Simple linear regression model** (one predictor, linear in parameters)
- **Linear in the parameters** (no $\beta^2$ or $\beta_1 \beta_0$ terms)
- **Linear in the predictor variable** ($X$ appears only as $X^1$)

For **simple linear regression** (one independent variable), the formal model is:

$Y_i = E[Y_i | X_i] + \epsilon_i$

Where:
* $Y_i$ is the $i$-th observed value of the dependent variable.
* $X_i$ is the $i$-th observed value of the independent variable.
* $E[Y_i | X_i]$ is the conditional mean of $Y$ for a given $X_i$, known as the **regression function**.
* $\epsilon_i$ is the random error term for the $i$-th observation.

Assuming a linear relationship for the regression function:

$E[Y_i | X_i] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$

Thus, the full simple linear regression model is:

$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i$

Where:
* $\beta_0$ (beta-nought) is the **Y-intercept**. It represents the mean value of $Y$ when $X$ is zero.
* $\beta_1$ (beta-one) is the **slope parameter**. It represents the change in the mean value of $Y$ for a one-unit increase in $X$.
* $\epsilon_i$ are typically assumed to be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables following a normal distribution with mean zero and constant variance: $\epsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$.

---

### Important Features of the Model

Let's break down what this model really means:

#### Feature 1: **The Response $Y_i$ Has Two Components**

$$Y_i = \underbrace{\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i}_{\text{systematic/deterministic}} + \underbrace{\epsilon_i}_{\text{random/error}}$$

1. **Constant term** $\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$: The systematic part
2. **Random term** $\epsilon_i$: The deviation from the systematic part

> 💡 **Interpretation**: Think of the regression line as the "signal" and the error term as the "noise."

**Why is this important?** Because of the random component $\epsilon_i$:
- $Y_i$ is a **random variable** 
- Different observations at the same X can have different Y values
- This is what creates the scatter in our plots

#### Feature 2: **Mean of $Y_i$ Equals the Regression Function**

Since $E\{\epsilon_i\} = 0$, we can derive:

$$E\{Y_i\} = E\{\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i\}$$

$$= \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + E\{\epsilon_i\}$$

$$= \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + 0$$

$$= \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$$

**Therefore, the regression function is:**

$$E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X \quad \text{(1.3)}$$

**What this means:**
- The regression function relates the *means* of the probability distributions of Y to the level of X
- At each X level, Y values are distributed around the regression line
- The regression line passes through the means of these distributions

> 💡 **Key insight**: Note that $\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$ plays the role of the constant $a$ in the general expected value formula $E\{a\} = a$.

#### Feature 3: **The Error Term Represents Deviations from the Mean**

The response $Y_i$ in the $i$-th trial exceeds or falls short of the regression function value by the error term amount $\epsilon_i$.

$$\epsilon_i = Y_i - (\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i) = Y_i - E\{Y_i\}$$

**Interpretation of $\epsilon_i$:**
- It's the vertical deviation of $Y_i$ from the regression line
- Positive $\epsilon_i$: observation is above the line
- Negative $\epsilon_i$: observation is below the line

![Error Term Illustration](placeholder-error-term-visual.png)

#### Feature 4: **Constant Variance (Homoscedasticity)**

All error terms $\epsilon_i$ have the same variance $\sigma^2$:

$$\sigma^2\{\epsilon_i\} = \sigma^2 \text{ for all } i$$

**Using the variance formula from Appendix A**:

$$\sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \sigma^2\{\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i\} = \sigma^2\{\epsilon_i\} = \sigma^2 \quad \text{(1.4)}$$

**What this means:**
- The probability distributions of Y have the **same variability** $\sigma^2$
- This is true regardless of the level of X
- The scatter around the regression line is uniform

> 📊 **Visual check**: In a well-fitting model, the vertical spread of points should be roughly constant as you move left to right on a scatter plot.

**Technical term**: This property is called **homoscedasticity** (constant variance).

> ⚠️ **Common violation**: **Heteroscedasticity** occurs when the variance changes with X. For example, variability might increase as X increases. We'll learn how to detect and handle this in later chapters.

#### Feature 5: **Uncorrelated Error Terms**

The error terms $\epsilon_i$ and $\epsilon_j$ are assumed to be uncorrelated:

$$\sigma\{\epsilon_i, \epsilon_j\} = 0 \text{ for all } i \neq j$$

**What this means:**
- The error in one observation doesn't affect the error in another
- Observations are independent
- Knowing $\epsilon_i$ tells us nothing about $\epsilon_j$

**Why is this important?** If errors are correlated, it often means:
- There's a pattern we haven't captured in the model
- The observations aren't truly independent (e.g., time series data)
- Our standard errors and confidence intervals will be wrong

> ⚠️ **Common violation**: Time series data often has **autocorrelated** errors - the error at time $t$ is correlated with the error at time $t-1$.

#### Feature 6: **Summary of Distributional Assumptions**

Regression model (1.1) implies that the responses $Y_i$ come from probability distributions with:

**Means**: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$
**Variances**: $\sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \sigma^2$ (same for all levels of X)
**Independence**: Any two responses $Y_i$ and $Y_j$ are uncorrelated

> 💡 **Big picture**: At each X level, Y values are randomly distributed around the regression line, with constant spread, and observations don't influence each other.

### Important Features of Model

Key features and assumptions of the simple linear regression model ($Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i$) often include:

1.  **Linearity:** The relationship between $X$ and the mean of $Y$ ($E[Y|X]$) is linear.
2.  **Normality of Errors:** The error terms $\epsilon_i$ are normally distributed.
3.  **Constant Variance (Homoscedasticity):** The variance of the error terms ($\sigma^2$) is constant across all levels of $X$. $\text{Var}(\epsilon_i) = \sigma^2$.
4.  **Independence of Errors:** The error terms $\epsilon_i$ are independent of each other.
5.  **X is Fixed or Measured Without Error:** $X$ values are either fixed constants or measured without significant error.
## Important Features of the Simple Linear Regression Model

The simple linear regression model is given by

$
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \varepsilon_i
$

The key features and assumptions of this model are as follows:

---

### 1️⃣ Linearity

The relationship between the predictor variable $X$ and the **mean** of the response variable $Y$ is linear. That is,

$
E(Y \mid X) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X
$

This assumption does **not** require individual observations to lie on a straight line, but rather that the **expected value** of $Y$ changes linearly with $X$.

---

### 2️⃣ Normality of Errors

The error terms $\varepsilon_i$ are assumed to be normally distributed:

$
\varepsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2)
$

This assumption implies that, for a fixed value of $X$, the response $Y$ follows a normal distribution. Normality is particularly important for:

- hypothesis testing,
- construction of confidence intervals, and
- prediction intervals.

---

### 3️⃣ Constant Variance (Homoscedasticity)

The error terms have constant variance for all levels of the predictor variable:

$
\{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma^2
$

This implies that the variability of the responses around the regression line is the same across all values of $X$. When this assumption is violated, the model exhibits **heteroscedasticity**.

---

### 4️⃣ Independence of Errors

The error terms are assumed to be independent of one another:

$
\{Cov}(\varepsilon_i, \varepsilon_j) = 0 \quad \text{for } i \neq j
$

As a consequence, the responses $Y_i$ and $Y_j$ are also uncorrelated. This assumption is especially important in time-series or sequential data, where dependence may naturally arise.

---

### 5️⃣ Zero Mean of Errors

The error terms have mean zero:

$
E(\varepsilon_i) = 0
$

This ensures that the regression function correctly represents the mean response of $Y$ for a given value of $X$.

---

### 6️⃣ Predictor Variable $X$ is Fixed or Measured Without Error

The values of the predictor variable $X$ are assumed to be:

- fixed constants chosen by the experimenter, or  
- measured without significant error.

This assumption implies that all randomness in the model is attributed to the response variable $Y$, not to $X$.

### Example: Electrical Distributor Bids

**📊 Example: Consultant Study of Bid Preparation Time**

A consultant is studying the relationship between:
- **X**: Number of bids prepared by construction contractors in a week
- **Y**: Number of hours required to prepare the bids

**The assumed regression model**:

$$Y_i = 9.5 + 2.1X_i + \epsilon_i$$

**Where:**
- $\beta_0 = 9.5$ hours (base time required)
- $\beta_1 = 2.1$ hours per bid
- $\epsilon_i$ = random error term

**The regression function**:

$$E\{Y\} = 9.5 + 2.1X$$

**Let's interpret this for a specific week:**

**Suppose in the $i$-th week, $X_i = 45$ bids are prepared.**

Then:
$$E\{Y_i\} = 9.5 + 2.1(45) = 9.5 + 94.5 = 104 \text{ hours}$$

**Suppose the actual time required is $Y_i = 108$ hours.**

Then the error term is:
$$\epsilon_i = Y_i - E\{Y_i\} = 108 - 104 = 4 \text{ hours}$$

**Interpretation:**
- The model predicts 104 hours for 45 bids
- The actual time was 108 hours
- The observation was 4 hours above the regression line
- This 4-hour deviation is the "error" or unexplained variation

![Probability Distribution at X = 45](placeholder-bid-example-distribution.png)

**Figure 1.6** shows:
- The probability distribution of Y when X = 45
- The distribution is centered at $E\{Y\} = 104$
- The actual observation $Y_i = 108$ came from this distribution
- The error term $\epsilon_i = +4$ is simply the deviation from the mean

**Another observation at X = 25 bids:**

The distribution would be centered at:
$$E\{Y\} = 9.5 + 2.1(25) = 9.5 + 52.5 = 62 \text{ hours}$$

**Key point**: Figure 1.6 shows that both distributions (at X = 25 and X = 45) have the *same variability* (same spread), conforming to the constant variance assumption.

### Meaning of Regression Parameters

The parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ in regression model (1.1) are called **regression coefficients**.

#### The Slope Parameter $\beta_1$

$$\beta_1 = \text{slope of the regression line}$$

**Interpretation**: $\beta_1$ indicates the **change in the mean** of the probability distribution of Y per unit increase in X.

**Formally:** The parameter $\beta_1$ is:
- The **Y-intercept** of the regression line
- The mean of the probability distribution of Y at X = 0

**📊 Example (continued): Electrical Distributor**

The regression function is:
$$E\{Y\} = 9.5 + 2.1X$$

**The slope** $\beta_1 = 2.1$ indicates:
- Preparing one additional bid per week leads to an increase of 2.1 hours in the mean time required
- This is the average effect across all weeks

![Slope Interpretation](placeholder-slope-illustration.png)

**Figure 1.7** illustrates:
- For a unit increase in X (one more bid)
- The mean of Y increases by $\beta_1 = 2.1$ hours
- This is the vertical rise for a horizontal run of 1

#### The Intercept Parameter $\beta_0$

$$\beta_0 = \text{Y-intercept of the regression line}$$

**Interpretation**: When the scope of the model includes X = 0, $\beta_0$ gives the mean of the probability distribution of Y at X = 0.

**📊 Example (continued): Electrical Distributor**

The intercept $\beta_0 = 9.5$ indicates the value of the regression function at X = 0.

**BUT WAIT** - there's a problem:

**The linear regression model was formulated for weeks where the number of bids prepared ranges from 20 to 80.**

**Therefore:**
- The scope does NOT include X = 0
- $\beta_0 = 9.5$ does NOT have an intrinsic meaning as a "base time"
- It's simply a mathematical constant needed to define the line

> ⚠️ **Common mistake**: Interpreting the intercept as meaningful when X = 0 is outside the scope of the model.

**When would $\beta_0$ be meaningful?**

If the scope were extended to include X levels near zero, AND we used a different model:
- A curvilinear regression function might be needed
- The value of $\beta_0$ would likely be different
- Only then could we interpret it as "base time when no bids are prepared"

> 💡 **Key principle**: When the scope of the model doesn't cover X = 0, the intercept $\beta_0$ is just a mathematical parameter without practical meaning. It's there to position the line correctly, not to be interpreted literally.

### Alternative Versions of Regression Model

Sometimes it's convenient to write the simple linear regression model in slightly different (but equivalent) forms.

#### Alternative Version 1: Associating an X Variable with the Intercept

Let $X_0$ be a constant identically equal to 1. Then we can write model (1.1) as:

$$Y_i = \beta_0 X_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i \quad \text{where } X_0 \equiv 1 \quad \text{(1.5)}$$

**Why do this?**
- Associates an X variable with each regression coefficient
- Useful in matrix notation and software implementation
- Makes formulas more symmetric and general

**Interpretation**: Think of it as:
- $\beta_0$ is the coefficient of a "predictor" that's always 1
- $\beta_1$ is the coefficient of the actual predictor X

#### Alternative Version 2: Using Deviations from the Mean

An alternative modification uses **deviations from the mean** of X rather than X itself:

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1(X_i - \bar{X}) + \beta_1\bar{X} + \epsilon_i$$

$$= (\beta_0 + \beta_1\bar{X}) + \beta_1(X_i - \bar{X}) + \epsilon_i$$

$$= \beta_0^* + \beta_1(X_i - \bar{X}) + \epsilon_i \quad \text{(1.6)}$$

**Where:**

$$\beta_0^* = \beta_0 + \beta_1\bar{X} \quad \text{(1.6a)}$$

**What changes?**
- The slope $\beta_1$ stays the same
- The intercept becomes $\beta_0^*$ instead of $\beta_0$
- The predictor is now $(X_i - \bar{X})$ instead of $X_i$

**Why do this?**
- Reduces multicollinearity in some applications
- Centers the model around the mean of X
- Can improve numerical stability in computations
- Makes $\beta_0^*$ interpretable as the expected Y at the mean of X

**Relationship between intercepts:**

$$\beta_0^* = \beta_0 + \beta_1\bar{X}$$

This means:
- $\beta_0$ is the Y-intercept when X = 0
- $\beta_0^*$ is the expected Y when X = $\bar{X}$ (the mean of X)

> 💡 **Practical note**: We use models (1.1), (1.5), and (1.6) **interchangeably** depending on convenience. They're mathematically equivalent - just different ways to write the same model.

## 1.4 Data for Regression Analysis

To estimate the regression parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ in model (1.1), we need data. But where does data come from, and what types of data can we use?

> 💡 **Reality check**: We usually don't know the true values of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ in advance. We need to **estimate** them from relevant data. Often we also don't know whether the regression relation is linear or curvilinear, so we rely on data analysis to develop a suitable model.

Data for regression analysis may come from two main sources:

### 1. Observational Data (Non-Experimental Studies)

**Observational data** come from studies where the predictor variable(s) are NOT controlled by the investigator.

**📊 Example: Age and Days of Illness**

Company officials want to study the relation between:
- **X**: Age of employee
- **Y**: Number of days of illness last year

**Data source**: Personnel records

**Characteristics:**
- The explanatory variable (age) is NOT controlled
- Data are observational since age cannot be assigned to employees
- The study is **non-experimental**

**Key limitation of observational data:**

> ⚠️ **Causation vs. Correlation**: Observational data often do NOT provide adequate information about cause-and-effect relationships.

**Why not?**

**Example**: Suppose we find a positive relation between age and number of days of illness:
- Does age *cause* more illness?
- Or do younger employees work indoors while older employees work outdoors?
- In that case, work location (confounding variable) might be the real cause
- Age and illness are correlated, but the causal pattern may be indirect

**The problem:**
- Older employees have *both* greater age AND outdoor work exposure
- We can't separate which factor causes more illness
- The two factors are **confounded**

> 💡 **Fundamental insight**: A positive regression relation between age and illness doesn't necessarily imply that an increase in age CAUSES faster writing speed. Other variables (education, parental factors) affect both vocabulary AND writing speed.

**Real underlying structure** (hypothetical):

```
Age of child ──────> Vocabulary (X)
                           ↑
Amount of parent's ─────┘
education

Age of child ──────> Writing speed (Y)
                           ↑
Amount of parent's ─────┘
education
```

**The confounding**: 
- Both X and Y are affected by age and parental education
- These common influences create correlation between X and Y
- But increasing vocabulary doesn't *cause* faster writing

**General principle:**

> ⚠️ **Whenever regression analysis is undertaken for purposes of description based on observational data, one should investigate whether explanatory variables other than those considered in the regression model might more directly explain cause-and-effect relationships.**

**When is observational data useful?**

Despite limitations, observational data are:
- **Frequently used** because controlled experimentation is often impractical
- **Valuable for description and control** purposes
- **Useful for prediction** even without understanding causation

In the company personnel example:
- It would not be possible to control age by assigning ages to employees
- Regression analysis with observational data is the only feasible approach
- The results can still inform administrative decisions

---

### 2. Experimental Data (Controlled Studies)

**Experimental data** come from studies where control over the explanatory/predictor variable(s) is exercised through **randomized assignments** to experimental units.

**Key concept**: When control over the explanatory variable(s) is exercised through **randomization**, the resulting **experimental data** provide much stronger information about cause-and-effect relationships than observational data.

#### Why Randomization Helps

**The benefit of randomization**: It tends to **balance out the effects** of any other variables that might affect the response variable.

**How it works:**
- Variables like aptitude affect productivity
- Random assignment ensures high-aptitude and low-aptitude analysts are *equally likely* to be assigned to any training length
- This balances out aptitude's effect across all treatment groups

**The result**: Randomization allows us to attribute differences in productivity to differences in training length, not to confounding factors.

> 💡 **Key insight**: The reason randomized experimental data can provide strong evidence about causation is that randomization tends to balance out the effects of all other factors.

#### Terminology of Experimental Design

**Treatment**: The different levels of the explanatory variable assigned to experimental units
- Example: Training lengths (2 weeks, 3 weeks, 5 weeks)

**Experimental units**: The entities to which treatments are assigned
- Example: The nine analysts in the productivity study

**Control over explanatory variable(s)**: Exercised through randomization
- Ensures each experimental unit has equal chance of receiving any treatment
- Isolates the effect of the treatment from other factors

---

#### Completely Randomized Design

The most basic type of experimental design for randomized treatment assignments is the **completely randomized design**.

**Definition**: Assignments are made **completely at random**, ensuring:
- All combinations of experimental units assigned to different treatments are equally likely
- Every experimental unit has an equal chance to receive any treatment

**📊 Example: Insurance Company Analyst Training**

An insurance company wants to study the relation between:
- **X**: Length of training (explanatory variable)
- **Y**: Productivity of analysts in processing claims (response variable)

**Study design:**
- **Treatments**: Three training lengths: 2 weeks, 3 weeks, 5 weeks
- **Experimental units**: Nine analysts to be used in study
- **Random assignment**:
  - Three analysts selected at random for 2-week training
  - Three analysts selected at random for 3-week training  
  - Three analysts selected at random for 5-week training
- **Control**: Random assignments made by randomization

**Complete randomization** provides that:
- All possible combinations of 9 analysts split into three groups of 3 are equally likely
- This includes 1,680 different possible assignments
- Each is equally likely under complete randomization

**Benefits of this design:**

1. **Balances confounding factors**: Any other variables affecting productivity (aptitude, prior experience, motivation) are randomly distributed across training groups

2. **Enables causal inference**: If productivity differs systematically by training length, we can attribute this to the training (not to confounding)

3. **Flexibility**: Permits different sample sizes for different treatments

4. **Efficiency**: Accommodates any number of treatments

**Chief disadvantage**: 
- When experimental units are heterogeneous (very different from each other)
- This design is not as efficient as some other designs
- We'll learn about more efficient designs in later chapters

> 💡 **Why randomization is powerful**: By randomly assigning treatments, we ensure that ALL other factors (known and unknown) are balanced on average across treatment groups. This isolates the treatment effect from confounding.

## 1.5 Overview of Steps in Regression Analysis

Now that we understand regression models and data sources, let's outline the typical strategy for conducting regression analysis.

### When Do We Know the Model in Advance?

Regression analysis can be used with:

## 1.6 Estimation of Regression Function

Now we get into the mechanics: How do we actually estimate the parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ from data?

### The Data

The data consist of observations on both the predictor variable X and the response variable Y.

**Notation**: For each trial (observation), we have:
- $(X_1, Y_1)$ for the first trial
- $(X_2, Y_2)$ for the second trial
- $(X_i, Y_i)$ for the $i$-th trial
- $(X_n, Y_n)$ for the $n$-th trial

Where:
- $i = 1, 2, \ldots, n$
- $n$ = total number of observations (sample size)

**📊 Example: Persistence Study**

In a small-scale study of persistence, an experimenter gave three subjects a very difficult task. Data on:
- **X**: Age of subject (years)
- **Y**: Number of attempts to accomplish the task before giving up

| Subject i | Age $X_i$ | Number of Attempts $Y_i$ |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1 | 20 | 5 |
| 2 | 55 | 12 |
| 3 | 30 | 10 |

**In notation:**
- $n = 3$ subjects
- Observations: $(X_1, Y_1) = (20, 5)$, $(X_2, Y_2) = (55, 12)$, $(X_3, Y_3) = (30, 10)$

---

### Method of Least Squares

To find "good" estimators of the regression parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$, we use the **method of least squares**.

#### The Core Idea

For observations $(X_i, Y_i)$, the method of least squares considers the **deviation** of $Y_i$ from its expected value:

$$Y_i - (\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i) \quad \text{(1.7)}$$

**What is this deviation?**
- It's the vertical distance from the observation $(X_i, Y_i)$ to the regression line
- It represents how far the actual Y value is from what the model predicts

#### The Least Squares Criterion

The method requires that we consider the **sum of the squared deviations**:

$$Q = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)^2 \quad \text{(1.8)}$$

**Why squared deviations?**
- Squaring makes all deviations positive (negative deviations don't cancel positive ones)
- Squaring penalizes large deviations more heavily than small ones
- Mathematical properties: leads to unique, tractable solutions

**The least squares estimators** of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are those values $b_0$ and $b_1$ that **minimize** the criterion Q for the given sample observations $(X_1, Y_1), (X_2, Y_2), \ldots, (X_n, Y_n)$.

> 💡 **Intuition**: We're finding the line that gets as close as possible (in a squared-distance sense) to all the data points simultaneously.

---

### Finding the Least Squares Estimators

There are two ways to find the values $b_0$ and $b_1$ that minimize Q:

#### Method 1: Numerical Search Procedures

- Systematically evaluate Q for different values of $b_0$ and $b_1$
- Find the combination that gives the minimum Q
- Illustrated in Figure 1.9 for the persistence study example

**📊 Example: Persistence Study Illustration**

Figure 1.9a shows:
- Data points: (20, 5), (55, 12), (30, 10)
- A trial regression line: $\hat{Y} = 9.0 + 0(X)$
  - This is a horizontal line at $\hat{Y} = 9.0$
  - Estimates: $b_0 = 9.0$, $b_1 = 0$
- Q = 26.0 (sum of squared vertical deviations)

Clearly this line doesn't fit well - the vertical deviations are large.

Figure 1.9b shows:
- A better regression line: $\hat{Y} = 2.81 + 0.177X$
  - Estimates: $b_0 = 2.81$, $b_1 = 0.177$
- Q = 5.7 (much smaller!)

This line fits much better - the squared deviations are minimized.

![Illustration of Least Squares Criterion](placeholder-least-squares-illustration.png)

**Calculating Q for the first case** $(b_0 = 9.0, b_1 = 0)$:

For subject 1: $(X_1, Y_1) = (20, 5)$
$$Y_1 - (b_0 + b_1 X_1) = 5 - [9.0 + 0(20)] = 5 - 9.0 = -4$$

Sum of squared deviations:
$$Q = (5-9.0)^2 + (12-9.0)^2 + (10-9.0)^2 = 16 + 9 + 1 = 26.0$$

**For the better line** $(b_0 = 2.81, b_1 = 0.177)$:

For subject 1:
$$Y_1 - (b_0 + b_1 X_1) = 5 - [2.81 + 0.177(20)] = 5 - 6.35 = -1.35$$

Sum of squared deviations:
$$Q = (5-6.35)^2 + (12-12.55)^2 + (10-8.12)^2 = 1.82 + 0.30 + 3.53 = 5.65 \approx 5.7$$

**The second line is much better**: Q is reduced from 26.0 to 5.7.

> 💡 **Key point**: The objective of the method of least squares is to find estimates $b_0$ and $b_1$ for which Q is a **minimum**. In a sense, these estimates provide a "good" fit of the regression line to the data.

#### Method 2: Analytical Procedures (Using Calculus)

When the regression model is not mathematically complex, we can use **analytical procedures** (calculus) to find the values that minimize Q.

**For regression model (1.1)**, the analytical approach is feasible. We can derive formulas for $b_0$ and $b_1$.

---

### The Normal Equations

Using calculus (taking partial derivatives of Q with respect to $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$, setting them to zero, and solving), we obtain the **normal equations**:

$$\sum Y_i = n b_0 + b_1 \sum X_i \quad \text{(1.9a)}$$

$$\sum X_i Y_i = b_0 \sum X_i + b_1 \sum X_i^2 \quad \text{(1.9b)}$$

**Terminology**: Equations (1.9a) and (1.9b) are called **normal equations**. The values $b_0$ and $b_1$ that satisfy them are called **point estimators** of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$, respectively.

> 📚 **Derivation** (optional): See the Comment section below if you're interested in how we derived these from calculus.

### Solving the Normal Equations

The normal equations (1.9) can be solved simultaneously for $b_0$ and $b_1$:

$$b_1 = \frac{\sum (X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sum (X_i - \bar{X})^2} \quad \text{(1.10a)}$$

$$b_0 = \frac{1}{n}\left(\sum Y_i - b_1 \sum X_i\right) = \bar{Y} - b_1 \bar{X} \quad \text{(1.10b)}$$

**Where:**
- $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$ is the mean of the X observations
- $\bar{Y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum Y_i$ is the mean of the Y observations

> 💡 **Important**: Computer calculations generally work with many digits to obtain accurate values for $b_0$ and $b_1$.

---

### Comment: Derivation of Normal Equations (Optional)

For those interested in the calculus behind the normal equations:

**Given sample observations** $(X_i, Y_i)$, the quantity Q in (1.8) is a function of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$. We find the values that minimize Q by:

1. **Taking partial derivatives** with respect to $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$:

$$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial \beta_0} = -2\sum(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)$$

$$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial \beta_1} = -2\sum X_i(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)$$

2. **Setting these equal to zero** and using $b_0$ and $b_1$ to denote the values of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ that minimize Q:

$$-2\sum(Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i) = 0$$

$$-2\sum X_i(Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i) = 0$$

3. **Simplifying** (divide by -2 and expand):

$$\sum Y_i - nb_0 - b_1\sum X_i = 0$$

$$\sum X_iY_i - b_0\sum X_i - b_1\sum X_i^2 = 0$$

4. **Rearranging** gives the normal equations (1.9).

> ✓ **Second derivative test**: A test of the second partial derivatives confirms that a minimum (not maximum) is obtained with the least squares estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$.

---

### Properties of Least Squares Estimators

The least squares estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ in (1.10) have several important **theoretical properties**:

**According to the Gauss-Markov theorem (1.11):**

> **Under the conditions of regression model (1.1), the least squares estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ in (1.10) are unbiased and have minimum variance among all unbiased linear estimators.**

Let's unpack what this means:

#### Property 1: **Unbiasedness**

The theorem (proven in the next chapter) states that $b_0$ and $b_1$ are **unbiased estimators**:

$$E\{b_0\} = \beta_0 \quad \text{and} \quad E\{b_1\} = \beta_1$$

**What does unbiased mean?**
- Neither estimator tends to overestimate or underestimate systematically
- On average (across many samples), they hit the true values
- The sampling distributions are centered at the true parameter values

> 💡 **Practical interpretation**: If we took many samples and calculated $b_1$ each time, the average of all those $b_1$ values would equal the true $\beta_1$.

#### Property 2: **Minimum Variance Among Linear Estimators**

The theorem also states that $b_0$ and $b_1$ have minimum variance among all **unbiased linear estimators**.

**What does this mean?**

**First, what's a linear estimator?**

The estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ are linear functions of the observations $Y_i$.

For instance, $b_1$ can be rewritten (shown in Chapter 2) as:

$$b_1 = \frac{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2} = \sum k_i Y_i$$

where:

$$k_i = \frac{X_i - \bar{X}}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$

**Since the $k_i$ are known constants** (because the $X_i$ are known), $b_1$ is a **linear combination** of the $Y_i$ and hence is a **linear estimator**.

**Similarly**, it can be shown that $b_0$ is also a linear estimator.

**Second, what's minimum variance?**

Among ALL unbiased linear estimators (there are infinitely many), $b_0$ and $b_1$ have the **smallest variability** (smallest variance).

**What this means practically:**
- Their sampling distributions are **less variable** (more precise) than other unbiased linear estimators
- They give us more reliable estimates
- They're the "best" linear unbiased estimators (BLUE)

> 💡 **Why this matters**: We want estimators that are both accurate (unbiased) AND precise (minimum variance). The Gauss-Markov theorem tells us that least squares estimators achieve both goals within the class of linear estimators.

We need to calculate $b_0$ and $b_1$ using formulas (1.10).

## 1.7 Point Estimation of Mean Response

Now that we have estimated the regression function, we can use it to estimate the mean response for any given level of X.

### Estimated Regression Function

Given sample estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$, we estimate the regression function (1.3) as:

$$\hat{Y} = b_0 + b_1 X \quad \text{(1.12)}$$

**Where**: 
- $\hat{Y}$ (read "Y hat") is the value of the **estimated regression function** at the level X of the predictor variable
- $b_0$ and $b_1$ are the least squares estimates from the sample

### Mean Response

**Terminology:**
- A **value** of the response variable $Y$ is just a single observation
- The **mean response** $E\{Y\}$ is the mean of the probability distribution of Y corresponding to a particular level X of the predictor

**The estimated mean response** $\hat{Y}$ is a **point estimator** of $E\{Y\}$.

**Why is it a good estimator?**

As an extension of the Gauss-Markov theorem (1.11), $\hat{Y}$ is:
- An **unbiased estimator** of $E\{Y\}$: $E\{\hat{Y}\} = E\{Y\}$
- Has **minimum variance** among all unbiased linear estimators of $E\{Y\}$

### Fitted Values

For each observation in the sample, we can calculate a **fitted value** $\hat{Y}_i$:

$$\hat{Y}_i = b_0 + b_1 X_i \quad \text{for } i = 1, 2, \ldots, n \quad \text{(1.13)}$$

**Important distinction:**
- $\hat{Y}_i$ = **fitted value** for the $i$-th case (predicted value from the model)
- $Y_i$ = **observed value** for the $i$-th case (actual data point)

> 💡 **Key point**: The fitted value $\hat{Y}_i$ estimates what we expect Y to be on average when X = $X_i$. It's not necessarily equal to the observed $Y_i$ because of random variation.

---

We found that the least squares estimates are:
$$b_0 = 62.37 \quad b_1 = 3.5702$$

**The estimated regression function is:**

$$\hat{Y} = 62.37 + 3.5702X$$

Our **point estimate** is simply:

- The mean labor time for lots of 65 units is about 294 hours

### Alternative Model (1.6)

When using the alternative regression model with deviations from the mean:

$$Y_i = \beta_0^* + \beta_1(X_i - \bar{X}) + \epsilon_i \quad \text{(1.6)}$$

**The least squares estimator of $\beta_1$ remains the same as before.**

**The least squares estimator of $\beta_0^*$** becomes:

$$b_0^* = b_0 + b_1\bar{X} = (\bar{Y} - b_1\bar{X}) + b_1\bar{X} = \bar{Y} \quad \text{(1.14)}$$

**Therefore, the estimated regression function in alternative form is:**

$$\hat{Y} = \bar{Y} + b_1(X - \bar{X}) \quad \text{(1.15)}$$

#### Example: Toluca Company Alternative Form

- $\bar{Y} = 312.28$ hours

$$\hat{Y} = 312.28 + 3.5702(X - 70.0)$$

$$\hat{Y}_1 = 312.28 + 3.5702(80 - 70.0) = 312.28 + 3.5702(10) = 312.28 + 35.70 = 347.98$$

This is, of course, identical to our earlier result using model (1.1).

> 💡 **Key point**: The two forms of the model give identical fitted values and predictions. They're just different ways of writing the same regression line.

## 1.8 Residuals

Residuals play a crucial role in regression analysis - they help us assess whether the model fits the data well.

### Definition of Residuals

The **$i$-th residual** is the difference between the observed value $Y_i$ and the corresponding fitted value $\hat{Y}_i$:

$$e_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i \quad \text{(1.16)}$$

**Alternative expression** (substituting the fitted value formula):

$$e_i = Y_i - (b_0 + b_1 X_i) = Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i \quad \text{(1.16a)}$$

### Residuals vs. Error Terms - CRITICAL DISTINCTION

It's essential to distinguish between:

#### **Error term** $\epsilon_i$ (model component):

- $\epsilon_i = Y_i - E\{Y_i\}$
- The vertical deviation of $Y_i$ from the **unknown true regression line**
- It's a theoretical quantity we can never actually observe
- Part of the model formulation

#### **Residual** $e_i$ (observable quantity):

- $e_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$
- The vertical deviation of $Y_i$ from the **estimated regression line** (fitted value)
- It's something we CAN calculate from our data
- Used to assess model fit

> ⚠️ **Why this matters**: 
> - The error term $\epsilon_i$ is unknown (we don't know the true line)
> - The residual $e_i$ is known (we can calculate it)
> - Residuals are highly useful for studying whether a given regression model is appropriate

**Figure 1.12** illustrates residuals graphically:

![Illustration of Residuals - Toluca Company](placeholder-residuals-illustration.png)

The figure shows:
- Observed values $(X_i, Y_i)$ as points
- Fitted values $\hat{Y}_i$ on the regression line
- Residuals $e_i$ as vertical line segments connecting observations to the line

**For the first two cases:**

**Case 1:** $(X_1, Y_1) = (80, 399)$
- Fitted value: $\hat{Y}_1 = 347.98$
- Residual: $e_1 = Y_1 - \hat{Y}_1 = 399 - 347.98 = 51.02$

**Case 2:** $(X_2, Y_2) = (30, 121)$
- Fitted value: $\hat{Y}_2 = 169.47$
- Residual: $e_2 = Y_2 - \hat{Y}_2 = 121 - 169.47 = -48.47$

**Interpretation:**
- $e_1 = 51.02 > 0$: The first observation is **above** the regression line by about 51 hours
- $e_2 = -48.47 < 0$: The second observation is **below** the regression line by about 48 hours

### Properties of the Fitted Regression Line

The estimated regression line (1.12) fitted by the method of least squares has several important properties. These follow directly from the least squares normal equations (1.9).

#### Property 1: **Sum of Residuals is Zero**

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} e_i = 0 \quad \text{(1.17)}$$

**What it means**: Positive and negative residuals balance out exactly.

**Why it's true**: This follows from the first normal equation (1.9a).

**Table 1.2, column 4** illustrates this for the Toluca Company example. The sum of residuals is 0 (rounding errors may cause slight deviations in practice).

> 💡 **Practical note**: In any particular dataset, rounding errors may result in the sum not equaling zero exactly, but it should be very close to zero.

#### Property 2: **Sum of Squared Residuals is Minimized**

$$\sum e_i^2 \text{ is a minimum}$$

**What it means**: The least squares estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ were chosen specifically to minimize $\sum e_i^2$.

**This was our criterion** when deriving the least squares estimators. The sum of squared residuals equals the criterion Q in (1.8) evaluated at the least squares estimates.

#### Property 3: **Sum of Observed Values Equals Sum of Fitted Values**

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{Y}_i \quad \text{(1.18)}$$

**What it means**: Total observed Y = Total fitted Y.

**Consequence**: The mean of the fitted values equals the mean of the observed values:

$$\bar{\hat{Y}} = \bar{Y}$$

**Table 1.2, columns 2 and 3** illustrates this for the Toluca Company example:
- $\sum Y_i = 7,807$
- $\sum \hat{Y}_i = 7,807$

#### Property 4: **Sum of Weighted Residuals is Zero (Weights = X)**

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i e_i = 0 \quad \text{(1.19)}$$

**What it means**: When each residual is weighted by the corresponding X value and summed, the result is zero.

**Why it's true**: This follows from the second normal equation (1.9b).

#### Property 5: **Sum of Weighted Residuals is Zero (Weights = Fitted Values)**

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{Y}_i e_i = 0 \quad \text{(1.20)}$$

**What it means**: When each residual is weighted by the corresponding fitted value and summed, the result is zero.

**Why it's true**: This is a consequence of properties (1.17) and (1.19).

#### Property 6: **The Regression Line Always Goes Through the Point** $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$

The regression line always passes through the point with coordinates $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$.

**Proof**: From the alternative form (1.15):

$$\hat{Y} = \bar{Y} + b_1(X - \bar{X})$$

When $X = \bar{X}$:

$$\hat{Y} = \bar{Y} + b_1(\bar{X} - \bar{X}) = \bar{Y} + b_1(0) = \bar{Y}$$

So the point $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$ satisfies the regression equation.

> 💡 **Intuitive explanation**: The least squares line must balance around the center of the data cloud, which is the point of means $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$.

---

### Comment: Proof of Properties

The six properties of the fitted regression line follow directly from the least squares normal equations (1.9). Here's a proof of Property 1 as an example:

**Property 1**: $\sum e_i = 0$

**Proof**:

From the definition of residuals:
$$e_i = Y_i - (b_0 + b_1 X_i)$$

Sum over all observations:
$$\sum e_i = \sum (Y_i - b_0 - b_1 X_i) = \sum Y_i - nb_0 - b_1\sum X_i$$

By the first normal equation (1.9a):
$$\sum Y_i = nb_0 + b_1\sum X_i$$

Therefore:
$$\sum e_i = (nb_0 + b_1\sum X_i) - nb_0 - b_1\sum X_i = 0$$

**Similar logic** can be used to prove the other properties.

## 1.9 Estimation of Error Terms Variance $\sigma^2$

The variance $\sigma^2$ of the error terms $\epsilon_i$ in regression model (1.1) needs to be estimated to:
1. Indicate the variability of the probability distributions of Y
2. Make various inferences concerning the regression function
3. Predict Y values

How do we estimate $\sigma^2$ from our data?

### Point Estimator of $\sigma^2$

To develop an estimator of $\sigma^2$ for regression model (1.1), we draw an analogy with estimating variance in simpler settings.

#### Analogy: Single Population

**Recall from basic statistics:**

For a **single population** with variance $\sigma^2$:
1. We estimate it using the **sample variance** $s^2$
2. To calculate $s^2$, we:
   - Compute each observation's deviation from the estimated mean $\bar{Y}$
   - Square these deviations
   - Sum them: $\sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$

**This sum is called a sum of squares.**

3. Divide by degrees of freedom: $\frac{\sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}{n-1}$

**Why $n-1$ degrees of freedom?**
- We lose one degree of freedom by using $\bar{Y}$ (an estimate of the unknown population mean $\mu$)

**The resulting estimator:**

$$s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}{n-1}$$

is an **unbiased estimator** of the population variance $\sigma^2$.

> 💡 **Key principle**: To estimate variance, we calculate squared deviations from a mean and divide by appropriate degrees of freedom.

#### Extension to Regression Model

**The logic is the same** for regression model (1.1):

**In regression:**
- The variance of each observation $Y_i$ is $\sigma^2$ (same as each error term $\epsilon_i$)
- We need to calculate squared deviations, but from what?

**Key difference**: 
- In the single population case, the $Y_i$ come from different probability distributions with different means that depend on $X_i$
- The mean of the distribution for observation $Y_i$ is $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$

**Therefore:**
- The deviation of $Y_i$ from its mean is: $Y_i - E\{Y_i\} = Y_i - (\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i)$
- But we don't know $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$, so we use estimates $b_0$ and $b_1$
- The estimated deviations are the **residuals**: $e_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$

**Appropriate sum of squares** (denoted SSE for "error sum of squares" or "residual sum of squares"):

$$SSE = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} e_i^2 \quad \text{(1.21)}$$

**Why this name?** SSE stands for:
- **Error** sum of squares, or
- **Residual** sum of squares

Both terms are used interchangeably.

### Degrees of Freedom for SSE

The sum of squares SSE has $n - 2$ degrees of freedom associated with it.

**Why $n - 2$?**
- We have $n$ observations
- We lose **two degrees of freedom** because:
  1. $\beta_0$ had to be estimated (by $b_0$)
  2. $\beta_1$ had to be estimated (by $b_1$)

**The estimated means** $\hat{Y}_i$ required estimating both regression parameters.

### Mean Square Error (MSE)

The appropriate **mean square** (variance estimate) is:

$$s^2 = MSE = \frac{SSE}{n-2} = \frac{\sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2}{n-2} = \frac{\sum e_i^2}{n-2} \quad \text{(1.22)}$$

**Where:** MSE stands for **error mean square** or **residual mean square**.

**Theoretical property** (proven in Chapter 2):

$$E\{MSE\} = \sigma^2 \quad \text{(1.23)}$$

**This means**: MSE is an **unbiased estimator** of $\sigma^2$ for regression model (1.1).

**Estimator of standard deviation** $\sigma$:

$$s = \sqrt{MSE}$$

This is simply the positive square root of MSE.

> 💡 **Interpretation**: $s$ (or $\sqrt{MSE}$) gives us an estimate of the typical size of the deviations of observations from the regression line.

---

### Example: Toluca Company (Continued)

**📊 Calculating SSE for the Toluca Company example**

Using the residuals from Table 1.2, column 4, and the squared residuals in column 5:

$$SSE = 54,825$$

**Degrees of freedom**: $n - 2 = 25 - 2 = 23$

**Mean square error (MSE)**:

$$s^2 = MSE = \frac{54,825}{23} = 2,384$$

**Standard deviation estimate**:

$$s = \sqrt{2,384} = 48.8 \text{ hours}$$

**Interpretation:**

**For the lot size X = 65 units**, we found earlier that:
- Mean work hours: $E\{Y\} \approx 294.4$ hours
- Standard deviation: $\sigma \approx 48.8$ hours

**What this tells us**:
- The estimated probability distribution of work hours for lots of 65 units has:
  - Mean: 294.4 hours
  - Standard deviation: 48.8 hours
- Variation from lot to lot for lots of 65 units is quite substantial (49 hours)
- This is about 17% of the mean (49/294 ≈ 0.17)

> 💡 **Practical insight**: Even for lots of the same size (65 units), the work hours can vary substantially due to factors not captured by lot size alone. The standard deviation of 48.8 hours quantifies this variability.

## 1.10 Normal Error Regression Model

So far, we've made no assumptions about the **form** of the distribution of the error terms $\epsilon_i$ (and hence of the responses $Y_i$).

To make **interval estimates** and perform **statistical tests**, however, we need to make an assumption about this distribution.

The standard assumption is that the error terms are **normally distributed**.

### The Model

The **normal error regression model** is:

$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i \quad \text{(1.24)}$$

**Where:**
- $Y_i$ is the observed response in the $i$-th trial
- $X_i$ is a known constant (the level of the predictor variable in the $i$-th trial)
- $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are parameters
- $\epsilon_i$ are **independent** $N(0, \sigma^2)$
- $i = 1, \ldots, n$

### What Changed?

**Compared to model (1.1):**

**Model (1.1)** assumed:
1. $E\{\epsilon_i\} = 0$
2. $\sigma^2\{\epsilon_i\} = \sigma^2$
3. Error terms are uncorrelated

**Model (1.24)** assumes:
1. All of the above, PLUS
2. The errors $\epsilon_i$ are **normally distributed**

**Why does normality matter?**
- Normality assumption greatly **simplifies** the theory of regression analysis
- It's **justifiable** in many real-world situations (as we'll explain)

### Comments on the Normal Error Model

#### 1. Notation: $N(0, \sigma^2)$

The symbol $N(0, \sigma^2)$ stands for:
- **Normally distributed**
- With **mean 0**
- With **variance $\sigma^2$**

#### 2. Same Model, More Specific Error Distribution

Model (1.24) is the **same** as regression model (1.1), except that model (1.24) assumes the errors $\epsilon_i$ are **normally distributed**.

**This has important consequences:**
- If errors are normal, the **assumption of uncorrelatedness** in model (1.1) becomes an **assumption of independence** in the normal error model
- Why? Because for normal random variables, zero correlation implies independence

> 💡 **Key point**: In the normal error model, uncorrelated errors are also independent errors.

#### 3. What Does Normal Error Model Imply About $Y_i$?

**Because** regression model (1.24) assumes that the errors $\epsilon_i$ are normally distributed, **it follows that**:

**The $Y_i$ are independent normal random variables**, with:

**Mean**: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$

**Variance**: $\sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \sigma^2$

**Figure 1.6** illustrates the normal error model:
- Each probability distribution of Y is **normally distributed**
- All have **constant variability** (same $\sigma^2$)
- The regression function is **linear**

![Normal Error Model Illustration](placeholder-normal-error-model.png)

#### 4. Implications of Independence Assumption

The regression model (1.24) assumes that the errors $\epsilon_i$ are normally distributed **and independent**.

**What this means:**
- The outcome in any trial has **no effect** on the error term for any other trial
- Whether $\epsilon_i$ is positive or negative, small or large, tells us nothing about $\epsilon_j$

**Example context**: In the Toluca Company example:
- Effects like **time lapse** since the production process was set up
- Machine **wear and tear**
- **Personnel changes** employed
- Could all vary **randomly** without reference to lot size
- Must vary **at random** (i.e., independently) from run to run for the independence assumption to hold

**Why independence matters**: If errors aren't independent, it often signals that we're missing important predictors or structure in our model.

#### 5. Justification for Normality Assumption

**Why is the normality assumption frequently justifiable?**

**Reason 1: Error term represents composite effects**

The error term $\epsilon_i$ represents the **composite effect** of many factors:
- Factors omitted from the model that affect Y
- Random variations in measurement
- Random fluctuations in Y

**Central Limit Theorem logic:**
- If many independent factors contribute to the error term
- The **composite effect** (sum of many small random effects) will tend toward normality
- This is true even if individual contributing factors aren't normally distributed

> 💡 **Intuition**: Just as sums of many random variables tend toward normality (by the Central Limit Theorem), error terms that represent many combined influences tend to be approximately normal.

**Example: Toluca Company**
- Effects such as time since setup, machine wear, personnel changes could vary independently
- The composite effect $\epsilon_i$ representing all these factors would tend toward normality

**Reason 2: Robustness of procedures**

**Even when the error distribution departs somewhat from normality:**
- The estimation and testing procedures to be discussed in the next chapter are usually only sensitive to **large departures** from normality
- Particularly with respect to **skewness**

**What this means:**
- Actual confidence coefficients and risks of errors will be **close to the levels for exact normality**
- Unless departures from normality are serious (especially skewness)

> ⚠️ **Practical note**: In practice, we should check the normality assumption (using residual plots and tests), but moderate departures often don't seriously affect our inferences.

## 1.11 Estimation of Parameters by Method of Maximum Likelihood

When we specify the functional form of the probability distribution of the error terms (e.g., normal distribution), we can use an alternative estimation method called **maximum likelihood**.

### What is Maximum Likelihood?

**Essentially**: The method of **maximum likelihood** chooses as estimates those values of the parameters that are **most consistent with the sample data**.

**How it works**:
- We calculate a "likelihood" of observing our data for different parameter values
- We choose the parameter values that make our observed data **most likely** (maximize the likelihood)

**General approach**: Let's first explain this method for a simpler case, then extend it to regression.

---

### Single Population Example

**Setup**: Consider a normal population with:
- Known standard deviation: $\sigma = 10$
- Unknown mean: $\mu$

**Sample**: A random sample of $n = 3$ observations yields:
$$Y_1 = 250, \quad Y_2 = 265, \quad Y_3 = 259$$

**Question**: Which value of $\mu$ is **most consistent** with the sample data?

**Two candidates**: Consider two possible values:
- $\mu = 230$
- $\mu = 259$

**Figure 1.13** shows the normal distributions with these two means:

![Densities for Sample Observations](placeholder-likelihood-example.png)

**(a) When $\mu = 230$**:

The three observations are in the **right tail** of the distribution.

**Densities at the observations**:
- $f_1 = f(Y_1 = 250 | \mu = 230) = 0.005399$
- $f_2 = f(Y_2 = 265 | \mu = 230) = 0.000087$
- $f_3 = f(Y_3 = 259 | \mu = 230) = 0.000595$

**Likelihood value** (product of densities):
$$L(\mu = 230) = f_1 \times f_2 \times f_3 = 0.005399 \times 0.000087 \times 0.000595$$
$$= 0.000000279 = 2.79 \times 10^{-9}$$

**Interpretation**: This is a **very small number**. The data are quite unlikely if $\mu = 230$.

**(b) When $\mu = 259$**:

The three observations are **near the center** of the distribution.

**Densities at the observations**:
- $f_1 = f(Y_1 = 250 | \mu = 259) = 0.026609$
- $f_2 = f(Y_2 = 265 | \mu = 259) = 0.033322$
- $f_3 = f(Y_3 = 259 | \mu = 259) = 0.039894$

**Likelihood value**:
$$L(\mu = 259) = f_1 \times f_2 \times f_3 = 0.026609 \times 0.033322 \times 0.039894$$
$$= 0.0000354 = 3.54 \times 10^{-5}$$

**Comparison**:

$$\frac{L(\mu = 259)}{L(\mu = 230)} = \frac{3.54 \times 10^{-5}}{2.79 \times 10^{-9}} \approx 12,700$$

> 💡 **Interpretation**: $L(\mu = 259)$ is much larger than $L(\mu = 230)$. In fact, the sample data are about **12,700 times more likely** if $\mu = 259$ than if $\mu = 230$.

**Conclusion**: $\mu = 259$ is much more consistent with the sample data than $\mu = 230$.

---

### The Likelihood Function

**Definition**: The **method of maximum likelihood** uses the **density** of $Y_i$ (i.e., the height of the probability curve at $Y_i$) as a measure of consistency for the observation $Y_i$.

**For a normal probability distribution** with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, the density function is (from Appendix A):

$$f_i = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_i - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^2\right]$$

**Density calculations**:

**When $\mu = 230$ and $\sigma = 10$:**

$$f_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(10)}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{250-230}{10}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{200\pi}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}(2)^2\right]$$
$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{200\pi}} \exp[-2] = 0.005399$$

Similarly:
$$f_2 = 0.000087, \quad f_3 = 0.000595$$

**When $\mu = 259$ and $\sigma = 10$:**

$$f_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(10)}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{250-259}{10}\right)^2\right] = 0.026609$$

Similarly:
$$f_2 = 0.033322, \quad f_3 = 0.039894$$

**The densities for all three observations for the two cases:**

| Observation | $\mu = 230$ | $\mu = 259$ |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| $f_1$ | 0.005399 | 0.026609 |
| $f_2$ | 0.000087 | 0.033322 |
| $f_3$ | 0.000595 | 0.039894 |

### The Likelihood Value

**The method of maximum likelihood** uses the **product of the densities** (i.e., the product of the three heights) as the measure of consistency of the parameter value $\mu$ with the sample data.

**This product is called the likelihood value** of the parameter value $\mu$, denoted by $L(\mu)$.

$$L(\mu) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f_i$$

**For our example with $\sigma = 10$:**

$$L(\mu) = \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(10)}}\right]^3 \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{250-\mu}{10}\right)^2\right] \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{265-\mu}{10}\right)^2\right] \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{259-\mu}{10}\right)^2\right]$$

**Simplifying:**

$$L(\mu) = \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(10)}}\right]^3 \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{250-\mu}{10}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{265-\mu}{10}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{259-\mu}{10}\right)^2\right]$$

> 💡 **Key concept**: If the value of $\mu$ is consistent with the sample data, the densities will be relatively large and so will be the product (the likelihood value). If the value of $\mu$ is not consistent with the data, the densities will be small, and the likelihood value will be small.

---

### Finding the Maximum Likelihood Estimate

**The maximum likelihood estimate** is that value of $\mu$ for which the likelihood value $L(\mu)$ is **largest (maximized)**.

**For our example**, Figure 1.14 shows a plot of the likelihood function $L(\mu)$ for many values of $\mu$:

![Likelihood Function for Estimation of Mean of Normal Population](placeholder-likelihood-function-plot.png)

**Key observations from the plot:**

1. **The likelihood function reaches a maximum at $\mu = 258$**.
   - This is the maximum likelihood estimate: $\hat{\mu} = 258$

2. **The likelihood values at $\mu = 230$ and $\mu = 259$ correspond to the ones calculated earlier**:
   - $L(\mu = 230) = 2.79 \times 10^{-9}$ (very small, unlikely)
   - $L(\mu = 259) = 3.54 \times 10^{-5}$ (much larger)

3. **The likelihood value at $\mu = 258$ is $L(\mu = 258) = 0.0000359$**, which is:
   - Slightly larger than $L(\mu = 259)$
   - About 12,900 times larger than $L(\mu = 230)$

> 💡 **Interpretation**: Among all possible values of $\mu$, $\hat{\mu} = 258$ is the value for which the likelihood of observing our sample ($Y_1 = 250$, $Y_2 = 265$, $Y_3 = 259$) is greatest.

**Why $\hat{\mu} = 258$ and not $\hat{\mu} = 259$?**

The maximum likelihood estimate is the **sample mean** $\bar{Y}$:

$$\hat{\mu} = \bar{Y} = \frac{250 + 265 + 259}{3} = \frac{774}{3} = 258$$

**General result**: For a normal population with known $\sigma$, the maximum likelihood estimator of $\mu$ is the sample mean $\bar{Y}$.

### Precision of Maximum Likelihood Estimate

**Note from the figure:**
- The likelihood function in Figure 1.14 is **relatively flat** in a fairly wide region around the maximum likelihood estimate $\hat{\mu} = 258$

**What this means:**
- For instance, for $\mu = 250$ or $\mu = 266$, the likelihood value is already only a little more than **one-half as large** as the likelihood value at $\mu = 258$

**Implication:**
- This indicates that the **maximum likelihood estimate here is relatively imprecise**
- Many values of $\mu$ not near the maximum likelihood estimate $\hat{\mu} = 258$ are almost as consistent with the sample data as the maximum likelihood estimate

**Why so imprecise?** 
- The sample size is very small ($n = 3$)
- With larger samples, the likelihood function becomes more sharply peaked around the maximum

> 💡 **General principle**: The sharper the peak of the likelihood function around the maximum, the more precise the estimate. Flat regions indicate uncertainty about the parameter value.

### General Formulation for Regression Model

**In general**, the density of an observation $Y_i$ for the normal error regression model (1.24) is:

$$f_i = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i}{\sigma}\right)^2\right] \quad \text{(1.25)}$$

**The likelihood function** for $n$ observations $Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_n$ is the product of the individual densities:

$$L(\beta_0, \beta_1, \sigma^2) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{(2\pi\sigma^2)^{1/2}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)^2\right]$$

$$= \frac{1}{(2\pi\sigma^2)^{n/2}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)^2\right] \quad \text{(1.26)}$$

**Note**: Since the variance $\sigma^2$ of the error terms is usually unknown, the likelihood function is a function of **three parameters**: $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$.

---

### Maximum Likelihood Estimators for Regression Model

**The values of $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$ that maximize the likelihood function** (1.26) are the **maximum likelihood estimators**, denoted by $\hat{\beta}_0$, $\hat{\beta}_1$, and $\hat{\sigma}^2$.

These estimators can be found analytically (using calculus). The results are:

| Parameter | Maximum Likelihood Estimator |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| $\beta_0$ | $\hat{\beta}_0 = b_0$ (same as (1.10b)) |
| $\beta_1$ | $\hat{\beta}_1 = b_1$ (same as (1.10a)) |
| $\sigma^2$ | $\displaystyle \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{\sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2}{n}$ |

**(1.27)**

**Key observations:**

1. **Maximum likelihood estimators of $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are the same as the least squares estimators**.
   - This is a reassuring result!
   - The two methods (least squares and maximum likelihood) lead to the same estimates for the regression coefficients

2. **Maximum likelihood estimator $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is biased**.
   - It differs slightly from the unbiased estimator MSE given in (1.22)
   - The ordinary unbiased estimator MSE or $s^2$ uses divisor $n - 2$
   - The maximum likelihood estimator uses divisor $n$

**Comparison of variance estimators:**

$$s^2 = MSE = \frac{n}{n-2}\hat{\sigma}^2 \quad \text{(1.28)}$$

**For small $n$**: The difference can be substantial.

**For large $n$**: The two estimators are approximately equal:
- As $n$ gets large, $\frac{n}{n-2} \to 1$
- So $MSE \approx \hat{\sigma}^2$

> 💡 **Practical note**: In practice, we typically use the unbiased estimator $s^2 = MSE$ rather than $\hat{\sigma}^2$, especially for small samples.

### Comments on Maximum Likelihood Estimators

#### Comment 1: Properties of Maximum Likelihood Estimators

Since the maximum likelihood estimators $\hat{\beta}_0$ and $\hat{\beta}_1$ are the **same** as the least squares estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$, they have **all the properties of least squares estimators**:

a. They are **unbiased**
b. They have **minimum variance** among all unbiased linear estimators

In addition, the maximum likelihood estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ for the normal error regression model (1.24) have **other desirable properties**:

c. They are **consistent** (as defined in A.52)
d. They are **sufficient** (as defined in A.53)
e. They are **minimum variance unbiased**; that is, they have minimum variance in the class of **all** unbiased estimators (linear or otherwise)

**Summary**: For the normal error model, the estimators $b_0$ and $b_1$ have many desirable properties.

#### Comment 2: Deriving Maximum Likelihood Estimators

**How do we find the values** of $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$ that maximize the likelihood function $L$ in (1.26)?

**Two methods:**

**Method 1: Numerical search**
- Evaluate $L$ for many combinations of parameter values
- Find the combination that gives the largest $L$

**Method 2: Analytical solution (using calculus)**
- Take partial derivatives of $L$ with respect to $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$
- Set them equal to zero
- Solve the resulting system of equations

**For some problems**, analytical solutions for maximum likelihood estimators are available.
**For others**, a computerized numerical search must be conducted.

**Mathematical simplification:**

We can work with $\log_e L$ (the natural logarithm of the likelihood function) rather than $L$ itself, because:
- Both $L$ and $\log_e L$ are maximized for the same values of $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$
- Taking logarithms simplifies the mathematics (products become sums)

**The log-likelihood function:**

$$\log_e L = -\frac{n}{2}\log_e 2\pi - \frac{n}{2}\log_e \sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)^2 \quad \text{(1.29)}$$

**Partial differentiation of the log-likelihood function** is much easier than differentiation of $L$ itself:

$$\frac{\partial(\log_e L)}{\partial\beta_0} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)$$

$$\frac{\partial(\log_e L)}{\partial\beta_1} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum X_i(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)$$

$$\frac{\partial(\log_e L)}{\partial\sigma^2} = -\frac{n}{2\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{2\sigma^4}\sum(Y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_i)^2$$

**Setting these equal to zero** (replacing $\beta_0$, $\beta_1$, and $\sigma^2$ by estimators $\hat{\beta}_0$, $\hat{\beta}_1$, and $\hat{\sigma}^2$) and simplifying:

$$\sum(Y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i) = 0 \quad \text{(1.30a)}$$

$$\sum X_i(Y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i) = 0 \quad \text{(1.30b)}$$

$$\frac{\sum(Y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i)^2}{n} = \hat{\sigma}^2 \quad \text{(1.30c)}$$

**Key result**:

> **Formulas (1.30a) and (1.30b) are identical to the earlier least squares normal equations (1.9)**, and formula (1.30c) is the biased estimator of $\sigma^2$ given earlier in (1.27).

### Problem 1.1

**Refer to the sales volume example on page 3. Suppose that the number of units sold is measured accurately, but clerical errors are frequently made in determining the dollar sales. Would the relation between the number of units sold and dollar sales still be a functional one? Discuss.**

**Answer:**

**No**, the relation would no longer be a functional one; it would become a **statistical relation**.

**Explanation:**

- **Functional relation** requires that every X value produces exactly one predictable Y value with no error
- In the original example: $Y = 2X$ exactly, because each unit sells for precisely $2
- **With clerical errors**: Even though the true relationship is $Y = 2X$, the *observed* Y values would include **measurement error**
- Therefore, the same X value could be associated with different observed Y values (due to recording mistakes)
- This creates **scatter** around the functional relationship, making it a **statistical relation**

> 💡 **Key insight**: Measurement error alone is sufficient to turn a functional relation into a statistical one, even if the underlying true relationship is deterministic.

Is it a functional relation or a statistical relation?**

**Mathematical expression:**

- There is **no randomness** or variability
- Every member with X visits pays exactly $Y = 300 + 2X$ dollars
- No scatter exists - all points would fall exactly on the line

**However**, if we were to collect actual data:
- There might be **clerical errors** in recording visits or charges
- Members might receive **discounts** or **penalties**
- Then the relation would become **statistical** due to these additional factors

**Why it seems like a functional relation:**
- The participant is correct that hardening is a "natural chemical process"

### Problem 1.5

**When asked to state the simple linear regression model, a student wrote it as follows: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i$. Do you agree?**

**Answer:**

**No, this is incorrect.**

**Why it's wrong:**

The student has confused the **regression function** with the **regression model**.

**Correct forms:**

**The regression model is:**
$$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i \quad \text{(Model)}$$

**The regression function (expected value) is:**
$$E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i \quad \text{(Function)}$$

**What's the mistake?**

- Taking the expected value: $E\{Y_i\} = E\{\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i\}$
- Since $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are constants and $X_i$ is known: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + E\{\epsilon_i\}$
- Since $E\{\epsilon_i\} = 0$ by assumption: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + 0 = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$

**Therefore:**
- The error term $\epsilon_i$ does NOT appear in the regression function
- $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$ (no $\epsilon_i$)

> 💡 **Key distinction**: 
> - **Model**: $Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i$ (individual observation, with error)
> - **Function**: $E\{Y_i\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i$ (mean of distribution, no error)

### Problem 1.7

**In a simulation exercise, regression model (1.1) applies with $\beta_0 = 100$, $\beta_1 = 20$, and $\sigma^2 = 25$. An observation on Y will be made for X = 5.**

**a. Can you state the exact probability that Y will fall between 195 and 205? Explain.**

**Answer:**

**No, we cannot state the exact probability.**

**Why not?**

- We don't know the **form of the distribution** of the error terms (and hence of Y)
- We only know:
  - $E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X = 100 + 20(5) = 200$
  - $\sigma^2\{Y\} = 25$, so $\sigma\{Y\} = 5$

**To calculate the exact probability** that $195 \leq Y \leq 205$, we would need to know the specific probability distribution (e.g., normal, uniform, etc.).

**What we CAN say:**
- Y is a random variable with mean 200 and standard deviation 5
- The interval [195, 205] is within ±1 standard deviation of the mean
- But without knowing the distribution, we can't calculate the exact probability

**b. If the normal error regression model (1.24) is applicable, can you now state the exact probability that Y will fall between 195 and 205? If so, state it.**

**Answer:**

**Yes, now we can calculate the exact probability.**

**Given:**
- The errors are normally distributed
- Therefore, Y is normally distributed
- $Y \sim N(200, 25)$, i.e., $Y \sim N(\mu = 200, \sigma = 5)$

**Standardize:**

$$P(195 \leq Y \leq 205) = P\left(\frac{195-200}{5} \leq \frac{Y-200}{5} \leq \frac{205-200}{5}\right)$$

$$= P(-1 \leq Z \leq 1)$$

where $Z \sim N(0,1)$ is standard normal.

**From standard normal table:**

$$P(-1 \leq Z \leq 1) = P(Z \leq 1) - P(Z \leq -1) = 0.8413 - 0.1587 = 0.6826$$

**Answer:** The probability that Y will fall between 195 and 205 is approximately **0.68 or 68%**.

> 💡 **Note**: This is the famous "68-95-99.7 rule" for normal distributions - about 68% of observations fall within ±1 standard deviation of the mean.
