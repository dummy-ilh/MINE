# Chapter 2: Inferences in Regression and Correlation Analysis

## Overview and Key Assumptions

This chapter covers **statistical inference** in regression analysis - how we draw conclusions about population parameters from sample data and make predictions.

> **Critical Assumption Throughout This Chapter**: We assume the **normal error regression model** (Equation 1.24) applies:
> 
> $$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i + \epsilon_i$$
> 
> where:
> - $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are unknown population parameters
> - $X_i$ are **known constants** (fixed predictor values)
> - $\epsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ independently (normal errors with constant variance)

**What you'll learn:**
1. Making inferences about the slope $\beta_1$ and intercept $\beta_0$
2. Estimating the mean response $E\{Y\}$ for a given $X$
3. Predicting individual new observations
4. Constructing confidence bands for the entire regression line
5. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) approach to regression
6. Measuring association strength: $R^2$ and correlation coefficient $r$

---

## 2.1 Inferences Concerning $\beta_1$ (The Slope)

### Why Care About $\beta_1$?

The slope $\beta_1$ tells us **how much $Y$ changes per unit change in $X$**. 

**Real-world example**: A marketing analyst studying sales ($Y$) vs. advertising expenditure ($X$) wants to know: "How many additional sales dollars, on average, are generated by each additional dollar of advertising?" This is $\beta_1$.

We're often interested in testing:
- $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$ (no linear relationship)
- $H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0$ (linear relationship exists)

**Why is $\beta_1 = 0$ special?** When $\beta_1 = 0$, the regression line is horizontal, meaning $X$ provides no information about $Y$. All probability distributions of $Y$ have the same mean regardless of $X$ value.

![When β₁ = 0, regression line is horizontal](placeholder-regression-beta1-zero.png)

**Figure 2.1 Interpretation**: When $\beta_1 = 0$, the regression line is horizontal at $E\{Y\} = \beta_0$. The probability distributions of $Y$ are identical and independent of $X$ - this means there's NO relationship between $X$ and $Y$.

---

### Sampling Distribution of $b_1$

The **point estimator** $b_1$ (our best guess for $\beta_1$ from sample data) was given earlier as:

$$b_1 = \frac{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.2)

The **sampling distribution** refers to what values $b_1$ would take if we repeatedly drew samples with the same $X$ values.

> **Sampling Distribution of $b_1$**: The distribution of $b_1$ values we'd get from many different samples, each with the same predictor values $X_1, X_2, ..., X_n$.

**Key Result**: For the normal error regression model, $b_1$ has a **normal distribution** with:

$$E\{b_1\} = \beta_1$$ (2.3a)

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.3b)

**What this means:**
- **Mean (2.3a)**: $b_1$ is **unbiased** - on average, it equals the true $\beta_1$
- **Variance (2.3b)**: The precision of $b_1$ improves when:
  - Error variance $\sigma^2$ is smaller (less noise)
  - $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ is larger (more spread in $X$ values)

---

### Why is $b_1$ a Linear Combination of the $Y_i$?

This technical detail is crucial for understanding why $b_1$ is normally distributed.

**Proof that $b_1 = \sum k_i Y_i$**:

Define constants:
$$k_i = \frac{X_i - \bar{X}}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.4a)

Then we can rewrite $b_1$ as:
$$b_1 = \sum k_i Y_i$$ (2.4)

**Useful Properties of the $k_i$ constants:**

$$\sum k_i = 0$$ (2.5)

$$\sum k_i X_i = 1$$ (2.6)

$$\sum k_i^2 = \frac{1}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.7)

> **Why these matter**: These properties help us prove that $b_1$ is unbiased and derive its variance. They also show that the least squares estimator has minimum variance among all unbiased linear estimators (Gauss-Markov theorem).

**Proof of (2.8)** - Showing $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y}) = \sum(X_i - \bar{X})Y_i$:

$$\sum(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y}) = \sum(X_i - \bar{X})Y_i - \sum(X_i - \bar{X})\bar{Y}$$

But $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})\bar{Y} = \bar{Y}\sum(X_i - \bar{X}) = 0$ since $\sum(X_i - \bar{X}) = 0$.

Therefore: $b_1 = \frac{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})Y_i}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2} = \sum k_i Y_i$ ✓

---

### Deriving the Mean and Variance of $b_1$

**Mean (Unbiasedness):**

$$E\{b_1\} = E\left\{\sum k_i Y_i\right\} = \sum k_i E\{Y_i\} = \sum k_i(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i)$$

$$= \beta_0 \sum k_i + \beta_1 \sum k_i X_i = \beta_0(0) + \beta_1(1) = \beta_1$$ ✓

**Variance:**

Since the $Y_i$ are independent with variance $\sigma^2$:

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = \sigma^2\left\{\sum k_i Y_i\right\} = \sum k_i^2 \sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \sum k_i^2 \sigma^2$$

$$= \sigma^2 \sum k_i^2 = \sigma^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ ✓

**Normality:**

> **Theorem A.40**: A linear combination of independent normal random variables is normally distributed.

Since each $Y_i \sim N(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_i, \sigma^2)$ independently, and $b_1 = \sum k_i Y_i$, it follows that $b_1$ is normally distributed.

---

### Estimated Variance of $b_1$

We don't know $\sigma^2$, so we estimate it using **MSE** (Mean Square Error):

$$s^2\{b_1\} = \frac{MSE}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.9)

where $MSE = \frac{SSE}{n-2}$ is our unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$.

The **estimated standard deviation** is: $s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{s^2\{b_1\}}$

> **Important**: $s\{b_1\}$ is also called the **standard error** of $b_1$. It measures the precision of our slope estimate.

---

### The t-Distribution for $b_1$

**Key Theorem (2.10)**: The standardized statistic

$$\frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{s\{b_1\}} \sim t(n-2)$$

follows a **t-distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom**.

**Why t-distribution instead of normal?**
- We're using $s\{b_1\}$ (estimated standard error) instead of $\sigma\{b_1\}$ (true standard error)
- This introduces additional uncertainty, which the t-distribution accounts for
- The $n-2$ degrees of freedom come from estimating two parameters ($\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$)

**Intuition**: If we knew $\sigma^2$, we could use the standard normal distribution. But since we estimate it from data, we lose 2 degrees of freedom (one for the mean, one for the variance), making the distribution slightly wider (heavier tails) than normal.

**Why $n-2$ specifically?** 
- We use $n$ observations
- We estimate 2 parameters ($\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$)  
- Degrees of freedom = $n - 2$
- This matches the degrees of freedom in $MSE = \frac{SSE}{n-2}$

---

### Confidence Interval for $\beta_1$

The $1-\alpha$ confidence interval for $\beta_1$ is:

$$b_1 \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{b_1\}$$ (2.15)

where $t(1-\alpha/2; n-2)$ is the $(1-\alpha/2) \times 100$ percentile of the t-distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom.

**Symmetry property** (2.13):
$$t(\alpha/2; n-2) = -t(1-\alpha/2; n-2)$$

This means the negative of the lower tail critical value equals the upper tail critical value.

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (95% Confidence Interval for $\beta_1$)

From the Toluca Company data (Table 2.1):
- $n = 25$ observations
- $\bar{X} = 70.00$
- $b_1 = 3.5702$
- $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = 19,800$
- $MSE = 2,384$

**Step 1**: Calculate $s^2\{b_1\}$

$$s^2\{b_1\} = \frac{MSE}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2} = \frac{2,384}{19,800} = 0.12040$$

$$s\{b_1\} = \sqrt{0.12040} = 0.3470$$

**Step 2**: Find critical value

For 95% confidence with $n-2 = 23$ df:
$$t(0.975; 23) = 2.069$$

**Step 3**: Construct interval

$$3.5702 \pm 2.069(0.3470)$$
$$3.5702 \pm 0.718$$
$$[2.85, 4.29]$$

**Interpretation**: With 95% confidence, we estimate that the mean number of work hours increases by somewhere between **2.85 and 4.29 hours** for each additional unit in the lot size.

> **Common Mistake**: Don't say "there's a 95% probability that $\beta_1$ is in this interval." The true $\beta_1$ is fixed (not random) - it's either in the interval or not. The randomness is in the interval itself, which varies from sample to sample. The correct interpretation: "If we repeated this sampling many times, 95% of the intervals we construct would contain the true $\beta_1$."

---

### Hypothesis Tests for $\beta_1$

Since $\frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{s\{b_1\}} \sim t(n-2)$, we can test hypotheses about $\beta_1$.

**Test Statistic:**
$$t^* = \frac{b_1}{s\{b_1\}}$$ (2.17)

(when testing $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$)

**Decision Rule** (for significance level $\alpha$):

$$\begin{align}
\text{If } |t^*| &\leq t(1-\alpha/2; n-2), \text{ conclude } H_0\\
\text{If } |t^*| &> t(1-\alpha/2; n-2), \text{ conclude } H_a
\end{align}$$ (2.18)

---

### 📊 Example 1: Two-Sided Test (Toluca Company)

**Question**: Is there a linear association between work hours and lot size?

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \beta_1 = 0$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0$$ (2.16)

**Test at $\alpha = 0.05$ level:**

**Step 1**: Calculate test statistic
$$t^* = \frac{b_1}{s\{b_1\}} = \frac{3.5702}{0.3470} = 10.29$$

**Step 2**: Decision rule
With $\alpha = 0.05$ and $df = 23$: $t(0.975; 23) = 2.069$

$$\begin{align}
\text{If } |t^*| &\leq 2.069, \text{ conclude } H_0\\
\text{If } |t^*| &> 2.069, \text{ conclude } H_a
\end{align}$$

**Step 3**: Conclusion
Since $|t^*| = 10.29 > 2.069$, we conclude $H_a$: there **IS a linear association** between work hours and lot size.

**P-value interpretation:**
$$P\text{-value} = P(|t| > 10.29) \approx 0.000$$

(shown as P = 0.000 in MINITAB output, Figure 2.2)

This extremely small P-value means the data would be incredibly unlikely if there were truly no relationship.

> **Note**: A small P-value tells us the association is **statistically significant**, not necessarily **practically important**. You still need to consider the magnitude of $b_1$ and the context of your problem.

---

### 📊 Example 2: One-Sided Test

**Question**: Is $\beta_1$ positive? (one-sided test)

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \beta_1 \leq 0$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 > 0$$

**Decision Rule** (for $\alpha = 0.05$):
$$\begin{align}
\text{If } t^* &\leq t(1-\alpha; n-2), \text{ conclude } H_0\\
\text{If } t^* &> t(1-\alpha; n-2), \text{ conclude } H_a
\end{align}$$

With $\alpha = 0.05$: $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$

Since $t^* = 10.29 > 1.714$, we conclude $H_a$: $\beta_1$ is positive.

> **Equivalence**: The one-sided P-value can be obtained from the two-sided P-value by dividing by 2. In Example 1, the two-sided P-value was 0.000, so the one-sided P-value is also essentially 0.

---

### Comments on P-Values

1. **The P-value is the observed level of significance** - it tells you the smallest $\alpha$ level at which you would reject $H_0$ with your data.

2. **Many publications report P-values** alongside test statistics. This lets readers make their own judgment about significance.

3. **Be careful with software output** - some packages report one-sided P-values (labeled PROB or P), others report two-sided. Always check!

4. **Testing $H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_{10}$ (specific nonzero value)**:

Sometimes we want to test if $\beta_1$ equals a specific value $\beta_{10}$ (e.g., historical norm, engineering specification, etc.).

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_{10}$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq \beta_{10}$$ (2.19)

**Test Statistic:**
$$t^* = \frac{b_1 - \beta_{10}}{s\{b_1\}}$$ (2.20)

The decision rule is identical to (2.18), just with $t^*$ defined by (2.20).

> **Note**: Test statistic (2.20) simplifies to (2.17) when $\beta_{10} = 0$.

## 2.2 Inferences Concerning $\beta_0$ (The Intercept)

### When Do We Care About $\beta_0$?

There are **infrequent occasions** when we want to make inferences about $\beta_0$. This only makes sense when the model scope includes $X = 0$.

> **Warning**: If the scope of the model doesn't extend to $X = 0$, inferences about $\beta_0$ may not be meaningful. The intercept represents the expected value of $Y$ when $X = 0$ - if $X = 0$ is outside the range of observed data, this is an extrapolation and may be unrealistic.

---

### Sampling Distribution of $b_0$

The **point estimator** for $\beta_0$ is:

$$b_0 = \bar{Y} - b_1 \bar{X}$$ (2.21)

For the normal error regression model, $b_0$ has a **normal distribution** with:

$$E\{b_0\} = \beta_0$$ (2.22a)

$$\sigma^2\{b_0\} = \sigma^2 \left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar{X}^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.22b)

**Key insights:**
- $b_0$ is **unbiased** (mean equals $\beta_0$)
- Like $b_1$, $b_0$ is a linear combination of the $Y_i$, so it's normally distributed
- The variance depends on:
  - Sample size $n$ (larger is better)
  - How far $\bar{X}$ is from 0 (closer to 0 gives smaller variance)
  - Spread of $X$ values (larger $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ is better)

**Estimated variance:**

$$s^2\{b_0\} = MSE\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar{X}^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.23)

$$s\{b_0\} = \sqrt{s^2\{b_0\}}$$

---

### The t-Distribution for $b_0$

**Theorem (2.24)**: The standardized statistic

$$\frac{b_0 - \beta_0}{s\{b_0\}} \sim t(n-2)$$

follows a t-distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom.

This allows us to construct confidence intervals and test hypotheses about $\beta_0$ using the same approach as for $\beta_1$.

---

### Confidence Interval for $\beta_0$

The $1-\alpha$ confidence interval for $\beta_0$ is:

$$b_0 \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{b_0\}$$ (2.25)

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (90% Confidence Interval for $\beta_0$)

**Important caveat**: The scope of the Toluca model doesn't extend to lot sizes of $X = 0$, so $\beta_0$ may not have intrinsic meaning here. But if we wanted a 90% confidence interval...

**Given:**
- $n = 25$, $\bar{X} = 70.00$, $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = 19,800$
- $b_0 = 62.37$, $MSE = 2,384$
- $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$

**Step 1**: Calculate $s^2\{b_0\}$

$$s^2\{b_0\} = MSE\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar{X}^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$

$$= 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{(70.00)^2}{19,800}\right]$$

$$= 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{4,900}{19,800}\right]$$

$$= 2,384[0.04 + 0.2475] = 685.34$$

$$s\{b_0\} = 26.18$$

**Step 2**: Construct interval

$$62.37 \pm 1.714(26.18)$$
$$62.37 \pm 44.87$$
$$[17.5, 107.2]$$

> **Interpretation**: With 90% confidence, we estimate that the expected work hours when lot size is zero (if meaningful) would be between 17.5 and 107.2 hours. But again, this extrapolation may not be meaningful since we don't have data near $X = 0$.

## 2.3 Some Considerations on Making Inferences Concerning $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$

### Effects of Departures from Normality

**Good news**: The confidence intervals and tests we've developed are **robust to moderate departures from normality**.

**Why?** Two reasons:

1. **Approximate normality**: Even if $Y$ isn't exactly normal, the **sampling distributions** of $b_0$ and $b_1$ will be approximately normal for sufficiently large samples (Central Limit Theorem).

2. **Asymptotic normality**: Both $b_0$ and $b_1$ have the property of **asymptotic normality** - their distributions approach normality as sample size increases, even if the errors are far from normal.

**Practical implication**: With sufficiently large samples, the confidence intervals and decision rules work well even if the probability distributions of $Y$ depart substantially from normality. For large samples, we can even replace $t$ values with $z$ values from the standard normal distribution.

> **Rule of thumb**: For moderate departures from normality, $n \geq 30$ is usually sufficient. For severe departures, you may need larger samples or transformation of variables (discussed in Chapter 3).

---

### Interpretation of Confidence Coefficient and Risks of Errors

**Confidence intervals for $\beta_1$** (with coefficient 0.95 in the Toluca example):

The interpretation assumes the $X_i$ are **known constants**. If we took many independent samples where:
- The $X$ observations are kept at the same levels as in the observed sample
- A 95% confidence interval is constructed for each sample

Then **95% of these intervals would contain the true $\beta_1$**.

**Important**: This interpretation is about **repeated sampling** with the same $X$ values, not about randomly selecting new predictor values.

> **Common misconception**: "There's a 95% probability that $\beta_1$ is in the interval." **Wrong!** The true $\beta_1$ is fixed. The interval is random. The correct interpretation: "95% of intervals constructed this way would capture $\beta_1$."

---

### Spacing of the $X$ Levels

**Key insight from formulas (2.3b) and (2.22b)**: The variances of $b_1$ and $b_0$ depend on the **spacing of the $X$ levels** in the observed data.

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$ (2.3b)

$$\sigma^2\{b_0\} = \sigma^2\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar{X}^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.22b)

**For given $n$ and $\sigma^2$:**
- **Greater spread in $X$ levels** → larger $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ → smaller variance of $b_1$
- The quantity $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ measures spread

**Implication**: In designed experiments where we can control the $X$ levels, we should space them widely to get more precise estimates!

> **We'll return to this in Chapter 4** on experimental design and optimal spacing of $X$ observations.

---

### Power of Tests

The **power** of a hypothesis test is the probability that we'll correctly reject $H_0$ when it's false (i.e., when $H_a$ is true).

For the general test (2.19):
$$H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_{10}$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq \beta_{10}$$

with test statistic (2.20) and decision rule (2.18), the power is:

$$\text{Power} = P\{|t^*| > t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \mid \delta\}$$ (2.26)

where $\delta$ is the **noncentrality measure**:

$$\delta = \frac{|\beta_1 - \beta_{10}|}{\sigma\{b_1\}}$$ (2.27)

**What this means:**
- $\delta$ measures how far the true $\beta_1$ is from the hypothesized $\beta_{10}$, in standard error units
- Larger $\delta$ → higher power (easier to detect the difference)
- Power increases as:
  - $|\beta_1 - \beta_{10}|$ increases (bigger true effect)
  - $\sigma\{b_1\}$ decreases (more precise estimates)

**Power tables**: Appendix Table B.5 gives power for two-sided t-tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ and $\alpha = 0.01$ for various degrees of freedom.

---

### 📊 Example: Power Calculation (Toluca Company)

**Question**: What's the power of the test when $\beta_1 = 1.5$?

**Setup:**
$$H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_{10} = 0$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq \beta_{10} = 0$$

**Given:**
- $\alpha = 0.05$
- $df = 23$
- Suppose $\sigma^2 = 2,500$ (planning value based on prior information)
- $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = 19,800$

**Step 1**: Calculate $\sigma^2\{b_1\}$

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2} = \frac{2,500}{19,800} = 0.1263$$

$$\sigma\{b_1\} = 0.3553$$

**Step 2**: Calculate noncentrality parameter

$$\delta = \frac{|1.5 - 0|}{0.3553} = 4.22$$

**Step 3**: Find power from Table B.5

- For $\alpha = 0.05$, $df = 23$
- $\delta$ between 4.00 and 5.00

Linear interpolation between $\delta = 4.00$ (power = 0.97) and $\delta = 5.00$ (power = 1.00):

$$0.97 + \frac{4.22 - 4.00}{5.00 - 4.00}(1.00 - 0.97) = 0.9766$$

**Conclusion**: If $\beta_1 = 1.5$, there's about a **98% probability** we'd correctly conclude there's a linear relation between work hours and lot size.

**Interpretation**: We'd be almost certain to detect a relationship this strong if it exists.

> **Power for one-sided tests**: For one-sided tests, use Table B.5 but enter with **one-half the significance level** shown in the table. For example, for a one-sided test at $\alpha = 0.05$, use the $\alpha = 0.10$ column (which represents $\alpha/2 = 0.05$ per tail in the two-sided test).

## 2.4 Interval Estimation of $E\{Y_h\}$ (Mean Response)

### Why Estimate the Mean Response?

A common objective in regression is to **estimate the mean for one or more probability distributions of $Y$**.

**Real-world examples:**

1. **Piecework pay study**: What's the mean productivity at high and medium pay levels? This helps analyze the benefits of increased pay.

2. **Toluca Company**: What's the mean number of work hours for a range of lot sizes? This helps find the optimum lot size.

3. **Company sales forecasting**: An economist estimates the regression of company sales on demographic variables (population 16+ years old). They want to predict next year's sales using demographic projections.

4. **College admissions**: An admissions officer estimates the regression of first-year college GPA on high school GPA. They want to predict the first-year GPA for an applicant with high school GPA of 3.5.

---

### Estimation vs. Prediction: A Critical Distinction

Let $X_h$ denote the level of $X$ for which we want to estimate the mean response $E\{Y_h\}$.

- $X_h$ may be a value from the sample, or
- $X_h$ may be some other value within the model scope

**The mean response when $X = X_h$** is denoted by $E\{Y_h\}$. Formula (1.12) gives the point estimator:

$$\hat{Y}_h = b_0 + b_1 X_h$$ (2.28)

**Two related but distinct problems:**

1. **Estimation of $E\{Y_h\}$** (mean response): Estimate the **mean** of the distribution of $Y$ at $X = X_h$. We predict the **mean** of many observations.

2. **Prediction of $Y_{h(\text{new})}$** (new observation): Predict an **individual outcome** drawn from the distribution of $Y$ at $X = X_h$. We predict a **single random draw**.

**Key difference:**
- Estimating the mean is less variable (the mean is fixed)
- Predicting an individual outcome has more uncertainty (includes both uncertainty about the mean AND random variation around the mean)

> **Intuition**: If you roll a die 100 times, you can estimate the mean outcome very precisely (close to 3.5). But predicting the next single roll has much more uncertainty (could be any value 1-6).

---

### Sampling Distribution of $\hat{Y}_h$

For the normal error regression model, $\hat{Y}_h$ has a **normal distribution** with:

$$E\{\hat{Y}_h\} = E\{Y_h\}$$ (2.29a)

$$\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sigma^2\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.29b)

**Key insights:**

1. **Normality**: Since $\hat{Y}_h = b_0 + b_1 X_h$ is a linear combination of $b_0$ and $b_1$ (which are themselves linear combinations of the normally distributed $Y_i$), $\hat{Y}_h$ is normally distributed.

2. **Unbiased**: $\hat{Y}_h$ is an unbiased estimator of $E\{Y_h\}$.

   **Proof:**
   $$E\{\hat{Y}_h\} = E\{b_0 + b_1 X_h\} = E\{b_0\} + X_h E\{b_1\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_h$$ 
   
   by (2.3a) and (2.22a). ✓

3. **Variance interpretation** (2.29b): The variability of $\hat{Y}_h$ is affected by how far $X_h$ is from $\bar{X}$.
   - When $X_h = \bar{X}$: variance is minimized at $\frac{\sigma^2}{n}$
   - When $X_h$ is far from $\bar{X}$: variance increases due to the term $\frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$

**Intuitive explanation**: Figure 2.3 shows two sample regression lines from samples with the same means $\bar{Y}$ and $\bar{X}$. 

- At $X_1$ (near $\bar{X}$): The fitted values $\hat{Y}_1$ from the two samples are close to each other
- At $X_2$ (far from $\bar{X}$): The fitted values $\hat{Y}_2$ differ substantially

![Variation in slope affects fitted values more at extreme X values](placeholder-yhat-variance.png)

**Why does this happen?** Variation in the estimated slope $b_1$ has a much more pronounced effect on $\hat{Y}_h$ when $X_h$ is far from the mean than when $X_h$ is near the mean. Think of the regression line pivoting around the point $(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$ - small rotations have big effects far from the pivot point!

---

### Estimated Variance and Standard Deviation of $\hat{Y}_h$

Substituting $MSE$ for $\sigma^2$ in (2.29b):

$$s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = MSE\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.30)

$$s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sqrt{s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}}$$

This is the **estimated standard deviation** (standard error) of $\hat{Y}_h$.

---

### Important Result: Uncorrelated Estimators

**Theorem (2.31)**: For regression model (2.1), $\bar{Y}$ and $b_1$ are **independent**.

$$\sigma\{\bar{Y}, b_1\} = 0$$ (2.31)

where $\sigma\{\bar{Y}, b_1\}$ denotes the covariance between $\bar{Y}$ and $b_1$.

**Proof:**

$$\bar{Y} = \sum\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)Y_i, \quad b_1 = \sum k_i Y_i$$

where $k_i = \frac{X_i - \bar{X}}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$

By (A.32) with $a_i = 1/n$ and $c_i = k_i$:

$$\sigma\{\bar{Y}, b_1\} = \sum\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)k_i\sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\sum k_i$$

But $\sum k_i = 0$ by (2.5). Hence the covariance is 0. ✓

**Why independence?** Since $\bar{Y}$ and $b_1$ are jointly normally distributed (as linear combinations of the normal $Y_i$) and have zero covariance, they are **independent** by Theorem (A.43).

**Now we can derive $\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$** using the alternative form (1.15):

$$\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sigma^2\{\bar{Y} + b_1(X_h - \bar{X})\}$$

Since $\bar{Y}$ and $b_1$ are independent, and $X_h$ and $\bar{X}$ are constants:

$$\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \sigma^2\{\bar{Y}\} + (X_h - \bar{X})^2\sigma^2\{b_1\}$$

Now $\sigma^2\{\bar{Y}\} = \frac{\sigma^2\{Y_i\}}{n} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$, and from (2.3b):

$$\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} + (X_h - \bar{X})^2 \cdot \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$

$$= \sigma^2\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$

which is (2.29b). ✓

---

### The t-Distribution for $\hat{Y}_h$

**Theorem (2.32)**: The standardized statistic

$$\frac{\hat{Y}_h - E\{Y_h\}}{s\{\hat{Y}_h\}} \sim t(n-2)$$

follows a t-distribution with $n-2$ degrees of freedom.

This follows from the same reasoning as for $b_1$ - we've encountered the t-distribution in every type of inference for regression model (2.1).

---

### Confidence Interval for $E\{Y_h\}$

The $1-\alpha$ confidence interval for $E\{Y_h\}$ is:

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.33)

---

### 📊 Example 1: Toluca Company (90% CI for Mean Response at $X_h = 65$)

**Question**: Find a 90% confidence interval for $E\{Y_h\}$ when lot size is $X_h = 65$ units.

**Given** (from Table 2.1):
- $n = 25$, $\bar{X} = 70.00$, $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = 19,800$
- $b_0 = 62.37$, $b_1 = 3.5702$
- $MSE = 2,384$
- $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$

**Step 1**: Calculate point estimate $\hat{Y}_h$

$$\hat{Y}_h = 62.37 + 3.5702(65) = 294.4$$

**Step 2**: Calculate $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$

$$s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{(65 - 70.00)^2}{19,800}\right]$$

$$= 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{25}{19,800}\right] = 98.37$$

$$s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 9.918$$

**Step 3**: Construct confidence interval

$$294.4 \pm 1.714(9.918)$$
$$294.4 \pm 17.0$$
$$[277.4, 311.4]$$

**Interpretation**: We conclude with confidence coefficient 0.90 that the **mean number of work hours** required when lots of 65 units are produced is somewhere between **277.4 and 311.4 hours**. Our estimate is moderately precise.

---

### 📊 Example 2: Toluca Company (90% CI for Mean Response at $X_h = 100$)

**Question**: Suppose we want to estimate $E\{Y_h\}$ for lots with $X_h = 100$ units.

**Step 1**: Calculate point estimate

$$\hat{Y}_h = 62.37 + 3.5702(100) = 419.4$$

**Step 2**: Calculate $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$

$$s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{(100 - 70.00)^2}{19,800}\right]$$

$$= 2,384\left[\frac{1}{25} + \frac{900}{19,800}\right] = 203.72$$

$$s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 14.27$$

**Step 3**: Construct confidence interval

$$419.4 \pm 1.714(14.27)$$
$$419.4 \pm 24.5$$
$$[394.9, 443.9]$$

**Comparison with Example 1**: This confidence interval is **somewhat wider** than for $X_h = 65$.

**Why?** The $X_h$ level here ($X_h = 100$) is substantially farther from the mean $\bar{X} = 70.0$ than the $X_h$ level in Example 1 ($X_h = 65$).

---

### Key Comments on Mean Response Estimation

**1. Since the $X_i$ are known constants** (in regression model 2.1), the interpretation of confidence intervals and risks of errors for mean response inferences is the same as for $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$: in terms of **repeated samples** where the $X$ observations are at the same levels as in the actual study.

**2. We see from formula (2.29b)** that for given sample results, the variance of $\hat{Y}_h$ is smallest when $X_h = \bar{X}$. Thus, in the experiment to estimate the mean response at a particular level $X_h$ of the predictor variable, **the precision of the estimate will be greatest if (everything else remaining equal) the observations on $X$ are spaced so that $\bar{X} = X_h$**.

**3. The usual relationship between confidence intervals and tests applies** in inferences concerning the mean response. The two-sided confidence limits (2.33) can be used for two-sided tests about $E\{Y_h\}$ at $X_h$. Alternatively, a regular decision rule can be set up.

**4. The confidence limits (2.33) for a mean response $E\{Y_h\}$** are not sensitive to moderate departures from the assumption that the error terms are normally distributed. The reasoning is the same as that for inferences on $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ (related to the robustness of estimating the mean response).

**5. Confidence limits (2.33) apply when a single mean response is to be estimated from the study.** We discuss in Chapter 4 how to proceed when several mean responses are to be estimated from the same data. (This involves **simultaneous inference** and **multiple comparisons**.)

## 2.5 Prediction of New Observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$

### The Prediction Problem

We now consider the prediction of a **new observation** $Y$ corresponding to a given level $X$ of the predictor variable.

**Three contexts where prediction is needed:**

1. **Toluca Company**: The next lot to be produced consists of 100 units, and management wishes to predict the number of work hours for this particular lot.

2. **Sales forecasting**: An economist has estimated the regression of company sales on demographic data for the past 10 years. Using a reliable demographic projection for the number of persons 16+ years old next year, the economist wishes to predict next year's company sales.

3. **College admissions**: An admissions officer has estimated the regression of first-year college GPA on high school GPA using admitted students. The officer wishes to predict the first-year college GPA for an applicant whose high school GPA is 3.5.

---

### Why Prediction is Harder Than Estimation

The **new observation** $Y$ to be predicted is viewed as the **result of a new trial**, independent of the trials on which the regression analysis is based.

**Let's denote:**
- $X_h$ = the level of $X$ for the new trial
- $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ = the new observation on $Y$ for this trial

The underlying regression model for the new observation is assumed to be **the same** as that applicable for the basic sample data. For instance, in the college admissions example:
- The basic sample data continues to be appropriate for the new observation
- The underlying regression relation still holds for the applicant

---

### Prediction Interval When Parameters Known

To illustrate the nature of a **prediction interval** for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ as simply as possible, let's first assume that all regression parameters are known.

Suppose the regression model for the college admissions example is known to be:

$$\beta_0 = 0.10, \quad \beta_1 = 0.95$$
$$E\{Y\} = 0.10 + 0.95X$$
$$\sigma = 0.12$$

An applicant has high school GPA $X_h = 3.5$. The mean college GPA for students with this average is:

$$E\{Y_h\} = 0.10 + 0.95(3.5) = 3.425$$

**Figure 2.4** shows the probability distribution of $Y$ for $X_h = 3.5$:
- Mean: $E\{Y_h\} = 3.425$
- Standard deviation: $\sigma = 0.12$
- Distribution is normal (by the regression model)

![Probability distribution of Y when Xₕ = 3.5](placeholder-prediction-known-params.png)

Suppose we want to predict that the college GPA of the applicant will be between:

$$E\{Y_h\} \pm 3\sigma$$
$$3.425 \pm 3(0.12)$$
$$[3.065, 3.785]$$

**What's the probability this prediction is correct?**

Since 99.7% of the area in a normal distribution falls within 3 standard deviations from the mean, the probability that this prediction interval will give a **correct prediction** for the applicant is 0.997.

---

### Key Idea of Prediction Intervals

> **Prediction interval idea**: Choose a range in the distribution of $Y$ wherein most of the observations will fall, and then declare that the next observation will fall in this range.

The usefulness of the prediction interval depends on:
- The **width** of the interval (narrower is better)
- The **user's needs for precision** (varies by application)

While prediction limits here are rather wide (applicant expected to attain at least 3.0 GPA in first year of college), the prediction interval **does indicate** to the admissions officer that the applicant is expected to perform well.

---

### Prediction Interval When Parameters Unknown

When the regression parameters are unknown, they must be estimated. The mean of the distribution of $Y$ is estimated by $\hat{Y}_h$, and the variance of the distribution of $Y$ is estimated by $MSE$.

**We cannot simply use prediction limits** (2.34):

$$E\{Y_h\} \pm z(1-\alpha/2)\sigma$$ (2.34)

with the parameters replaced by corresponding point estimators. **Why not?**

---

### Understanding the Prediction Error

When the regression parameters are unknown and point estimators are **substituted**, the prediction limits become:

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm z(1-\alpha/2)\sqrt{MSE}$$

But this would **understate** the true uncertainty!

**The problem**: There are **TWO sources of variation** when predicting $Y_{h(\text{new})}$:

1. **Variation of $Y$ at $X = X_h$** (intrinsic randomness): $\sigma^2$
2. **Variation of the sampling distribution of $\hat{Y}_h$** (estimation uncertainty): $\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$

Figure 2.5 illustrates this intuitively. Two probability distributions of $Y$ are shown:
- One if $E\{Y_h\}$ is at the left confidence limit
- One if $E\{Y_h\}$ is at the right confidence limit

![Two sources of uncertainty in prediction](placeholder-prediction-two-sources.png)

Since we don't know the mean $E\{Y_h\}$ and only estimate it by a confidence interval, we cannot be certain of the **location** of the distribution of $Y$. The prediction limits for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ **must take account of BOTH elements**:

1. Variation in possible location of the distribution of $Y$
2. Variation within the probability distribution of $Y$

---

### Sampling Distribution of the Prediction Error

**Theorem (2.35)**: Prediction limits for a new observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ at a given level $X_h$ are obtained by means of:

$$\frac{Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h}{s\{\text{pred}\}} \sim t(n-2)$$ (2.35)

**What is $s\{\text{pred}\}$?**

The **estimated standard deviation of the prediction**, $s\{\text{pred}\}$, is defined shortly in equation (2.37). The studentized statistic (2.35) uses $\hat{Y}_h$ in the numerator (not the true mean $E\{Y_h\}$) because the true mean is unknown and cannot be used in making a prediction.

**From theorem (2.35)**, the $1-\alpha$ prediction limits for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ are:

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{\text{pred}\}$$ (2.36)

---

### Understanding the Prediction Variance

The numerator of (2.35) represents how far the new observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ will deviate from the estimated mean $\hat{Y}_h$ based on the original $n$ cases in the study. This difference may be viewed as the **prediction error**, with $\hat{Y}_h$ serving as the best point estimate of $Y_{h(\text{new})}$.

The variance of this prediction error can be readily obtained by using the independence of $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ and the original $n$ sample cases on which $\hat{Y}_h$ is based:

$$\sigma^2\{\text{pred}\} = \sigma^2\{Y_{h(\text{new})} - \hat{Y}_h\} = \sigma^2\{Y_{h(\text{new})}\} + \sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ 

$$= \sigma^2 + \sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.37)

**Breaking down the variance:**

$$\sigma^2\{\text{pred}\} = \sigma^2 + \sigma^2\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$

$$= \sigma^2\left[1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$

**Note that $\sigma^2\{\text{pred}\}$ has TWO components:**

1. **$\sigma^2\{Y_{h(\text{new})}\} = \sigma^2$**: The variance of the distribution of $Y$ at $X = X_h$ (intrinsic randomness)

2. **$\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$**: The variance of the sampling distribution of $\hat{Y}_h$ (estimation uncertainty)

---

### Estimated Prediction Variance

An unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2\{\text{pred}\}$ is:

$$s^2\{\text{pred}\} = MSE + s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.38)

which can be expressed using (2.30) as:

$$s^2\{\text{pred}\} = MSE\left[1 + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.38a)

$$s\{\text{pred}\} = \sqrt{s^2\{\text{pred}\}}$$

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (90% Prediction Interval for Next Lot)

**Question**: The Toluca Company wants to predict the work hours for the **next lot** of $X_h = 100$ units, with a 90% prediction interval.

**Given** (from earlier work):
- $\hat{Y}_h = 419.4$
- $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 203.72$
- $MSE = 2,384$
- $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$

**Step 1**: Calculate $s^2\{\text{pred}\}$

$$s^2\{\text{pred}\} = MSE + s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$
$$= 2,384 + 203.72 = 2,587.72$$

$$s\{\text{pred}\} = 50.87$$

**Step 2**: Construct prediction interval

$$419.4 \pm 1.714(50.87)$$
$$419.4 \pm 87.2$$
$$[332.2, 506.6]$$

**Interpretation**: With 90% confidence, we predict that the number of work hours for the next production run of 100 units will be somewhere between **332 and 507 hours**.

---

### Comments on Prediction Intervals

**1. The 90% prediction interval for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ is wider than the 90% confidence interval for $E\{Y_h\}$ (Example 2 on page 55)**

This is expected, since both of the prediction intervals are wider than the 90% confidence interval for $E\{Y_h\}$ obtained in Example 2 on page 55.

**Why wider?**
- **Prediction interval**: $s\{\text{pred}\} = 50.87$ (large because it includes intrinsic randomness)
- **Confidence interval for mean**: $s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 14.27$ (smaller, only estimation error)

**2. Formula (2.38a) indicates that the prediction interval is wider the further $X_h$ is from $\bar{X}$.**

The reason is that the estimate of the mean $\hat{Y}_h$ is less precise the further $X_h$ is from $\bar{X}$.

**3. Prediction intervals resemble confidence intervals, but differ conceptually.**

- **Confidence interval**: An inference on a **parameter** and is an interval that is intended to cover the value of the parameter
- **Prediction interval**: A statement about the value to be taken by a **random variable**, the new observation $Y_{h(\text{new})}$

**4. The confidence coefficient for prediction limits (2.36) refers to taking repeated samples based on the same set of $X$ values**, and calculating prediction limits for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ for each sample.

**5. Prediction limits (2.36) apply for a single prediction based on the sample data.**

> **Next in Chapter 4**: We'll discuss how to predict the mean of $m$ new observations for a given $X_h$, and in Chapter 4 we take up how to make several predictions from the same data.

**6. The prediction limits for a new observation are not sensitive to moderate departures from normality** (assuming the sample size is adequate).

**7. The prediction interval can still be useful for control purposes even when it's wide.**

In the Toluca example, the 90% prediction interval (332 to 507 hours) was rather wide and may not be too useful for planning worker requirements for the next lot. 

**However**, the interval can still be useful for **control** purposes. For instance, suppose that the actual work hours on the next lot of 100 units were 550 hours. Since this falls **outside the prediction limits**, management would have an indication that a change in the production process may have occurred and would **be alerted** to the possible need for remedial action.

> **Important application**: Wide prediction intervals are still valuable for **detecting outliers** or **process changes**!

---

### Additional Insight: Primary Reason for Wide Prediction Intervals

**Note**: The primary reason for the wide prediction interval is the **large lot-to-lot variability** in work hours for any given lot size.

$$MSE = 2,384 \text{ accounts for 92% of the estimated prediction variance } s^2\{\text{pred}\} = 2,587.72$$

**Interpretation**: About 92% of prediction uncertainty comes from the intrinsic randomness ($\sigma^2$), not from estimation uncertainty ($\sigma^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$).

**Implications:**

It may be that the large lot-to-lot variability reflects other factors that affect the required number of work hours besides lot size, such as:
- Amount of experience of employees assigned to the lot production
- Time pressure to complete a lot quickly
- Quality of raw materials

A **multiple regression model** incorporating these other factors might lead to much more precise predictions. Alternatively, a **designed experiment** could be conducted to determine the main factors leading to the large lot-to-lot variation. A **quality improvement program** could then use these findings to achieve more uniform performance (e.g., by additional training of employees if inadequate training accounted for much of the variability).

## 2.6 Prediction of Mean of $m$ New Observations for Given $X_h$

### The Problem

Occasionally, one would like to predict the **mean of $m$ new observations on $Y$** for a given level of the predictor variable.

**Example**: Suppose the Toluca Company has been asked to bid on a contract that calls for $m = 3$ production runs of $X_h = 100$ units during the next few months. Management would like to predict the **mean work hours per lot** for these three runs and then convert this into a prediction of the **total work hours** required to fill the contract.

---

### Prediction Limits for Mean of $m$ New Observations

Let $\bar{Y}_{h(\text{new})}$ denote the mean of the $m$ new $Y$ observations to be predicted. It can be shown that the appropriate $1-\alpha$ prediction limits are, assuming the new $Y$ observations are independent:

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{\text{predmean}\}$$ (2.39)

where:

$$s^2\{\text{predmean}\} = \frac{MSE}{m} + s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.39a)

or equivalently:

$$s^2\{\text{predmean}\} = MSE\left[\frac{1}{m} + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X_h - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]$$ (2.39b)

**Note from (2.39a)**: The variance $s^2\{\text{predmean}\}$ has **two components**:

1. **$\frac{MSE}{m}$**: The variance of the mean of $m$ observations from the probability distribution of $Y$ at $X = X_h$ (intrinsic variability of the mean)

2. **$s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$**: The variance of the sampling distribution of $\hat{Y}_h$ (estimation uncertainty)

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (90% Prediction Interval for Mean of 3 Lots)

**Question**: Find the 90% prediction interval for the mean number of work hours $\bar{Y}_{h(\text{new})}$ in three new production runs, each for $X_h = 100$ units.

**Given** (from previous work):
- $\hat{Y}_h = 419.4$
- $s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 203.72$
- $MSE = 2,384$
- $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$

**Step 1**: Calculate $s^2\{\text{predmean}\}$

$$s^2\{\text{predmean}\} = \frac{MSE}{m} + s^2\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$

$$= \frac{2,384}{3} + 203.72 = 998.4$$

$$s\{\text{predmean}\} = 31.60$$

**Step 2**: Construct prediction interval for mean work hours per lot

$$419.4 \pm 1.714(31.60)$$
$$419.4 \pm 54.2$$
$$[365.2, 473.6]$$

**Step 3**: Convert to total work hours for three lots

Multiply by 3:

$$3(365.2) \leq \text{Total work hours} \leq 3(473.6)$$

$$1,095.6 \leq \text{Total work hours} \leq 1,420.8$$

**Interpretation**: We predict with 90% confidence that between **1,096 and 1,421 work hours** will be needed to fill the contract for three lots of 100 units each.

---

### Comment on Prediction Intervals

**The 90% prediction interval for $\bar{Y}_{h(\text{new})}$ is narrower than the prediction interval for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ on page 59**

This is expected, since both of the prediction intervals are wider than that obtained for $Y_{h(\text{new})}$ on page 59, as expected.

**Why?**
- **Prediction interval for individual observation**: $s\{\text{pred}\} = 50.87$
- **Prediction interval for mean of 3 observations**: $s\{\text{predmean}\} = 31.60$

The prediction interval for the **mean** is narrower because the mean of multiple observations has less variability than a single observation.

**Furthermore**, both prediction intervals are wider than the 90% confidence interval for $E\{Y_h\}$ obtained in Example 2 on page 55 ($s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 14.27$), also as expected.

## 2.6 Confidence Band for Regression Line

### Why Do We Need a Confidence Band?

Sometimes we'd like to obtain a **confidence band for the entire regression line** $E\{Y\} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X$. This band enables us to see:
- The region in which the entire regression line lies
- How precisely we've estimated the regression function

This is particularly useful for determining the **appropriateness of a fitted regression function**, as we'll explain in Chapter 3.

---

### The Working-Hotelling Confidence Band

The **Working-Hotelling $1-\alpha$ confidence band** for the regression line has boundary values at any level $X_h$:

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm W \cdot s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.40)

where:

$$W^2 = 2F(1-\alpha; 2, n-2)$$ (2.40a)

and $\hat{Y}_h$ and $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ are defined in (2.28) and (2.30).

**Key observation**: The formula for the boundary values is **exactly the same form** as the confidence limits for the mean response at $X_h$ (formula 2.33):

$$\hat{Y}_h \pm t(1-\alpha/2; n-2) \cdot s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$$ (2.33)

**except** the $t$ multiple has been replaced by the $W$ multiple.

---

### Why Use $W$ Instead of $t$?

**The $W$ multiple must be larger than the $t$ multiple** because:
- The confidence band must encompass the **entire regression line** (infinitely many points)
- Confidence limits for a mean response at $X_h$ apply only **at that single level** $X_h$

Consequently, the **boundary points of the confidence band** for the regression line are **wider apart** the further $X_h$ is from the mean $\bar{X}$ of the $X$ observations.

**Why?** Because:
- The confidence band must encompass the entire regression line
- Whereas the confidence limits for $E\{Y_h\}$ at $X_h$ apply only at the single level $X_h$

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (90% Confidence Band)

**Question**: Determine how precisely we've estimated the regression function for the Toluca Company example by obtaining the 90% confidence band for the regression line.

**Step 1**: Find $W$

$$W^2 = 2F(1-\alpha; 2, n-2) = 2F(0.90; 2, 23) = 2(2.549) = 5.098$$

$$W = 2.258$$

**Step 2**: Calculate boundary values at $X_h = 100$

From earlier work:
- $\hat{Y}_h = 419.4$
- $s\{\hat{Y}_h\} = 14.27$

Boundary values:
$$419.4 \pm 2.258(14.27)$$
$$419.4 \pm 32.2$$
$$[387.2, 451.6] \text{ at } X_h = 100$$

**Step 3**: Calculate boundary values for other $X_h$ levels

We can calculate $\hat{Y}_h$ and $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ for each $X_h$ level from (2.28) and (2.30), then find the boundary values using (2.40).

**Figure 2.6** contains a plot of the confidence band for the regression line.

![90% confidence band for Toluca Company regression line](placeholder-confidence-band.png)

**Interpretation**: At $X_h = 100$, the boundary values are 387.2 and 451.6. We see that the regression line for the Toluca Company example has been **estimated fairly precisely**. The slope of the regression line is clearly positive, and the levels of the regression line at different levels of $X$ are **estimated fairly precisely except for small and large lot sizes**.

---

### Comments on Confidence Bands

**1. The boundary values of the confidence band for the regression line in (2.40) define a hyperbola.**

This can be seen by replacing $\hat{Y}_h$ and $s\{\hat{Y}_h\}$ by their definitions in (2.28) and (2.30):

$$b_0 + b_1 X \pm W\sqrt{MSE}\left[\frac{1}{n} + \frac{(X - \bar{X})^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\right]^{1/2}$$ (2.41)

**2. The boundary values of the confidence band for the regression line at any value $X_h$ are often NOT substantially wider than the confidence limits for the mean response at that single $X_h$ level.**

In the Toluca Company example:
- $t$ multiple for estimating the mean response at $X_h = 100$: $t(0.95; 23) = 1.714$
- Confidence interval: $(394.9, 443.9)$

vs.

- $W$ multiple for the **entire regression line**: $W = 2.258$
- Confidence band at $X_h = 100$: $(387.2, 451.6)$

With confidence coefficient 0.90, the **$W$ multiple** for the entire regression line is only about **32% larger** than the $t$ multiple in (2.33).

This comparison suggests that one is able to draw conclusions about **any and all mean responses** for the entire regression line and not just about the mean response at a given $X$ level. Some uses of this broader base for inference will be explained in the next two chapters.

**3. The confidence band (2.40) applies to the entire regression line over all real-numbered values of $X$ from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$.**

The confidence coefficient indicates the proportion of times that the estimation procedure will yield a band that covers the **entire line**, in a long series of samples in which the $X$ observations are kept at the same level as in the actual study.

**In applications**, the confidence band is ignored for that part of the regression line which is **not of interest** in the problem at hand. 

In the Toluca Company example:
- Negative lot sizes would be ignored
- The confidence coefficient for a limited segment of the band is somewhat higher than $1-\alpha$
- So $1-\alpha$ serves as a **lower bound** to the confidence coefficient

**4. Some alternative procedures for developing confidence bands for the regression line have been developed.**

The simplicity of the Working-Hotelling confidence band (2.40) arises from the fact that it is a **direct extension** of the confidence limits for a single mean response in (2.33).

## 2.7 Analysis of Variance Approach to Regression Analysis

We now consider regression analysis from the perspective of **analysis of variance (ANOVA)**. This new perspective:
- Won't enable us to do anything new
- **Will** come into its own when we take up multiple regression models and other types of linear statistical models

The ANOVA approach is based on **partitioning sums of squares** and **degrees of freedom** associated with the response variable $Y$.

---

### Partitioning of Total Sum of Squares

#### Basic Notions

To explain the analysis of variance approach, consider again the **Toluca Company example**.

**Figure 2.7a** shows the observations $Y_i$ for the first two production runs (Table 1.1).

![Illustration of partitioning total deviations](placeholder-anova-partition.png)

Disregarding lot sizes, we see **variation** in the number of work hours $Y_i$, as in all statistical data. This variation is conveniently measured in terms of the **deviations**:

$$Y_i - \bar{Y}$$ (2.42)

These deviations are shown by the vertical lines in Figure 2.7a. The measure of **total variation**, denoted by **SSTO**, is the sum of the squared deviations (2.42):

$$SSTO = \sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$$ (2.43)

**SSTO** stands for **total sum of squares**:
- If all $Y_i$ observations are the same, $SSTO = 0$
- The greater the variation among the $Y_i$ observations, the larger is $SSTO$

Thus, $SSTO$ for our example is a **measure of the uncertainty** in predicting $Y$ (work hours required for a lot) when the lot size is **not taken into account**.

---

### When We Utilize the Predictor Variable $X$

When we utilize the predictor variable $X$, the variation in $Y_i$ is that of the $Y_i$ observations **around the fitted regression line**:

$$Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$$ (2.44)

These deviations are shown by the vertical lines in Figure 2.7b. The measure of variation in the $Y_i$ observations that is present when the predictor variable $X$ is taken into account is the **sum of the squared deviations** (2.44), which is the familiar **SSE** of (1.21):

$$SSE = \sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2$$ (2.45)

**SSE** denotes **error sum of squares**:
- If all $Y_i$ observations fall on the fitted regression line, $SSE = 0$
- The greater the variation of the $Y_i$ observations around the fitted regression line, the larger is $SSE$

For the Toluca Company example (Table 2.1):

$$SSTO = 307,203, \quad SSE = 54,825$$

**What accounts for the substantial difference?** The difference is another sum of squares:

$$SSR = \sum(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2$$ (2.46)

where **SSR** stands for **regression sum of squares**.

Note that $SSR$ is a sum of squared deviations:

$$\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}$$ (2.47)

These deviations are shown by the vertical lines in Figure 2.7c. Each deviation is simply **the difference between the fitted value** on the regression line and **the mean** of the fitted values $\bar{Y}$.

> **Recall from (1.18)**: The mean of the fitted values $\hat{Y}_i$ is $\bar{Y}$. If the regression line is horizontal so that $b_1 = 0$ and $\hat{Y}_i \equiv \bar{Y}$, then $SSR = 0$. Otherwise, $SSR$ is positive.

---

### $SSR$ as a Measure of Variability

$SSR$ may be considered a **measure of that part of the variability** of the $Y_i$ which is **associated with the regression line**. The larger $SSR$ is in relation to $SSTO$, the **greater is the effect** of the regression relation in accounting for the total variation in the $Y_i$ observations.

For the Toluca Company example:

$$SSR = SSTO - SSE = 307,203 - 54,825 = 252,378$$

This indicates that **most of the total variability** in work hours is accounted for by the relation between lot size and work hours.

---

### Formal Development of Partitioning

The **total deviation** $Y_i - \bar{Y}$, used in the measure of total variation of the observations $Y_i$ without taking the predictor variable into account, can be **decomposed into two components**:

$$\underbrace{Y_i - \bar{Y}}_{\text{Total deviation}} = \underbrace{\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}}_{\substack{\text{Deviation of fitted} \\ \text{regression value} \\ \text{around mean}}} + \underbrace{Y_i - \hat{Y}_i}_{\substack{\text{Deviation around} \\ \text{fitted regression} \\ \text{line}}}\quad$$ (2.48)

The two components are:

1. **$\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}$**: The deviation of the fitted value around the mean $\bar{Y}$
2. **$Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$**: The deviation of the observation $Y_i$ around the fitted regression line

**Figure 2.7** shows this decomposition for observation $Y_1$ by the broken lines.

---

### Remarkable Property: Sum of Squared Deviations

It's a **remarkable property** that the sums of these squared deviations have the same relationship:

$$\sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2 = \sum(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 + \sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2$$ (2.49)

or, using the notation in (2.43), (2.45), and (2.46):

$$SSTO = SSR + SSE$$ (2.50)

---

### Proof of the Partitioning (2.50)

$$\sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2 = \sum[(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}) + (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)]^2$$

$$= \sum[(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 + (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 + 2(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)]$$

$$= \sum(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 + \sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2 + 2\sum(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)$$

**The last term equals zero**, as we can see by expanding it:

$$2\sum(\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i) = 2\sum\hat{Y}_i(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i) - 2\bar{Y}\sum(Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)$$

- The first summation equals zero by (1.20)
- The second equals zero by (1.17)

Hence, (2.49) follows. ✓

---

### Comment on Alternative Formula for $SSR$

The formulas for $SSTO$, $SSR$, and $SSE$ in (2.43), (2.45), and (2.46) are best for **computational accuracy**.

**Alternative formulas** that are algebraically equivalent are available. One that is useful for **deriving analytical results** is:

$$SSR = b_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$ (2.51)

---

## Breakdown of Degrees of Freedom

### Understanding Degrees of Freedom

Corresponding to the partitioning of the total sum of squares $SSTO$, there is a partitioning of the **associated degrees of freedom** (abbreviated $df$).

We have $n-1$ degrees of freedom associated with $SSTO$. Why?

> **One degree of freedom is lost** because the deviations $Y_i - \bar{Y}$ are subject to **one constraint**: they must sum to zero. Equivalently, one degree of freedom is lost because the sample mean $\bar{Y}$ is used to estimate the population mean.

$SSE$ has $n-2$ degrees of freedom associated with it. Why?

> **Two degrees of freedom are lost** because the two parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ are estimated in obtaining the fitted values $\hat{Y}_i$.

$SSR$ has **one degree of freedom** associated with it. Why?

> Although there are $n$ deviations $\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}$, all fitted values $\hat{Y}_i$ are calculated from the **same estimated regression line**. Two degrees of freedom are associated with a regression line, corresponding to the intercept and the slope of the line.

> **One of the two degrees of freedom** is lost because the deviations $\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y}$ are subject to a constraint: they must sum to zero.

**Note**: The degrees of freedom are **additive**:

$$n - 1 = 1 + (n-2)$$

For the Toluca Company example:

$$24 = 1 + 23$$

---

## Mean Squares

### Definition of Mean Squares

A sum of squares divided by its associated degrees of freedom is called a **mean square** (abbreviated $MS$).

For instance, an ordinary sample variance is a mean square since a sum of squares, $\sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$, is divided by its associated degrees of freedom, $n-1$.

We are interested here in the **regression mean square**, denoted by $MSR$:

$$MSR = \frac{SSR}{1} = SSR$$ (2.52)

and in the **error mean square**, $MSE$, defined earlier in (1.22):

$$MSE = \frac{SSE}{n-2}$$ (2.53)

For the Toluca Company example:

$$MSR = \frac{252,378}{1} = 252,378$$

$$MSE = \frac{54,825}{23} = 2,384$$

---

### Comment: Mean Squares Are NOT Additive

The two mean squares $MSR$ and $MSE$ do **not** add to:

$$\frac{SSTO}{n-1} = \frac{307,203}{24} = 12,800$$

Thus, **mean squares are not additive**.

## Expected Mean Squares

### Why Do We Need Expected Mean Squares?

To make inferences based on the analysis of variance approach, we need to know the **expected value** of each of the mean squares. The expected value of a mean square is the **mean of its sampling distribution** and tells us **what is being estimated** by the mean square.

Statistical theory provides the following results:

$$E\{MSE\} = \sigma^2$$ (2.56)

$$E\{MSR\} = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$ (2.57)

The expected mean squares in (2.56) and (2.57) are shown in the analysis of variance table in Table 2.2.

> **Note**: Result (2.56) is in accord with our earlier statement that $MSE$ is an **unbiased estimator** of $\sigma^2$.

---

### Two Important Implications

**1. The mean of the sampling distribution of $MSE$ is $\sigma^2$ whether or not $X$ and $Y$ are linearly related, i.e., whether or not $\beta_1 = 0$.**

This follows directly from (2.56).

**2. The mean of the sampling distribution of $MSR$ is also $\sigma^2$ when $\beta_1 = 0$.**

From (2.57), when $\beta_1 = 0$:
$$E\{MSR\} = \sigma^2 + 0 = \sigma^2$$

Hence, when $\beta_1 = 0$, the sampling distributions of $MSR$ and $MSE$ are **located identically** and $MSR$ and $MSE$ will tend to be of the **same order of magnitude**.

**On the other hand, when $\beta_1 \neq 0$**, the mean of the sampling distribution of $MSR$ is greater than $\sigma^2$ since the term $\beta_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ in (2.57) must then be positive.

Thus, when $\beta_1 \neq 0$, the mean of the sampling distribution of $MSR$ is **located to the right** of that of $MSE$, and hence $MSR$ will tend to be **larger than $MSE$**.

---

### Using the Expected Mean Squares

This suggests that a **comparison** of $MSR$ and $MSE$ is useful for testing whether or not $\beta_1 = 0$.

- If $MSR$ and $MSE$ are of the **same order of magnitude**, this would suggest that $\beta_1 = 0$
- If $MSR$ is **substantially greater** than $MSE$, this would suggest that $\beta_1 \neq 0$

This indeed is the **basic idea** underlying the analysis of variance test to be discussed next.

---

### Comment: Derivation of $E\{MSR\}$

The derivation of (2.56) follows from theorem (2.11), which states that $SSE/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2$ with $n-2$ degrees of freedom and is independent of $b_0$ and $b_1$.

Hence, it follows from property (A.42) of the chi-square distribution:

$$E\left\{\frac{SSE}{\sigma^2}\right\} = n - 2$$

or equivalently:

$$E\left\{\frac{SSE}{n-2}\right\} = E\{MSE\} = \sigma^2$$

---

**To find the expected value of $MSR$**, we begin with (2.51):

$$SSR = b_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$

Now by (A.15a):

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = E\{b_1^2\} - (E\{b_1\})^2$$ (2.58)

We know from (2.3a) that $E\{b_1\} = \beta_1$ and from (2.3b) that:

$$\sigma^2\{b_1\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}$$

Hence, substituting into (2.58):

$$E\{b_1^2\} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2} + \beta_1^2$$

It now follows that:

$$E\{SSR\} = E\{b_1^2\} \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$

Finally, $E\{MSR\}$ is:

$$E\{MSR\} = E\left\{\frac{SSR}{1}\right\} = \sigma^2 + \beta_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$

---

## $F$ Test of $\beta_1 = 0$ versus $\beta_1 \neq 0$

The analysis of variance approach provides us with a **battery of highly useful tests** for regression models (and other linear statistical models). For the simple linear regression case considered here, the analysis of variance provides us with a test for:

$$H_0: \beta_1 = 0$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0$$ (2.59)

---

### Test Statistic

The test statistic for the analysis of variance approach is denoted by $F^*$. As just mentioned, it **compares $MSR$ and $MSE$** in the following fashion:

$$F^* = \frac{MSR}{MSE}$$ (2.60)

The earlier motivation, based on the expected mean squares in Table 2.2, suggests that **large values of $F^*$** support $H_a$ and **values of $F^*$ near 1** support $H_0$. In other words, the appropriate test is an **upper-tail one**.

---

### Sampling Distribution of $F^*$

To construct a statistical decision rule, we need to know the sampling distribution of $F^*$ when $H_0$ ($\beta_1 = 0$) holds.

**Cochran's theorem** will be most helpful in this connection. For our purposes, this theorem can be stated as follows:

> **Theorem (2.61)**: If all $n$ observations $Y_i$ come from the same normal distribution with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, and $SSTO$ is decomposed into $k$ sums of squares $SS_r$, each with degrees of freedom $df_r$, then the $SS_r/\sigma^2$ terms are **independent $\chi^2$ variables** with $df_r$ degrees of freedom if:
> 
> $$\sum_{r=1}^{k} df_r = n - 1$$

Note from Table 2.2 that we have decomposed $SSTO$ into the **two sums of squares** $SSR$ and $SSE$ and that their degrees of freedom are additive. Hence:

> **If $\beta_1 = 0$** so that all $Y_i$ have the same mean $\mu = \beta_0$ and the same variance $\sigma^2$, then $SSE/\sigma^2$ and $SSR/\sigma^2$ are **independent $\chi^2$ variables**.

---

Now consider test statistic $F^*$, which we can write as:

$$F^* = \frac{SSR}{\sigma^2} \div \frac{SSE}{\sigma^2} = \frac{MSR}{MSE}$$

But by Cochran's theorem, when $H_0$ holds:

$$F^* \sim \frac{\chi^2(1)}{1} \div \frac{\chi^2(n-2)}{n-2}$$

when $H_0$ holds.

where the $\chi^2$ variables are **independent**. Thus, when $H_0$ holds, $F^*$ is the ratio of two **independent $\chi^2$ variables**, each divided by its degrees of freedom. But this is the definition of an $F$ random variable in (A.47).

**We have thus established that if $H_0$ holds, $F^*$ follows the $F$ distribution**, specifically the $F(1, n-2)$ distribution.

When $H_a$ holds, it can be shown that $F^*$ follows the **noncentral $F$ distribution**, a complex distribution that we need not consider further at this time.

---

### Comment

Even if $\beta_1 \neq 0$, $SSR$ and $SSE$ are independent and $SSE/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2$. However, the condition that both $SSR/\sigma^2$ and $SSE/\sigma^2$ are $\chi^2$ random variables requires $\beta_1 = 0$.

---

### Construction of Decision Rule

Since the test is **upper-tail** and $F^*$ is distributed as $F(1, n-2)$ when $H_0$ holds, the decision rule for controlling the risk of a Type I error at $\alpha$ is:

$$\begin{align}
\text{If } F^* &\leq F(1-\alpha; 1, n-2), \text{ conclude } H_0\\
\text{If } F^* &> F(1-\alpha; 1, n-2), \text{ conclude } H_a
\end{align}$$ (2.62)

where $F(1-\alpha; 1, n-2)$ is the $(1-\alpha) \times 100$ percentile of the appropriate $F$ distribution.

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company ($F$ Test)

For the Toluca Company example, we shall repeat the earlier test on $\beta_1$, this time using the $F$ test. The alternative conclusions are:

$$H_0: \beta_1 = 0$$
$$H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0$$

As before, let $\alpha = 0.05$. Since $n = 25$, we require $F(0.95; 1, 23) = 4.28$.

**Decision rule:**

$$\begin{align}
\text{If } F^* &\leq 4.28, \text{ conclude } H_0\\
\text{If } F^* &> 4.28, \text{ conclude } H_a
\end{align}$$

We have from earlier that $MSR = 252,378$ and $MSE = 2,384$. Hence:

$$F^* = \frac{252,378}{2,384} = 105.9$$

Since $F^* = 105.9 > 4.28$, we conclude $H_a$: that $\beta_1 \neq 0$, or that there **IS** a linear association between work hours and lot size. This is the same result as when the $t$ test was employed, as it must be according to our discussion below.

**The MINITAB output** in Figure 2.2 on page 46 shows the $F^*$ statistic in the column labeled F. Next to it is shown the **P-value**, $P\{F(1, 23) > 105.9\}$, namely, $0+$, indicating that the data are not consistent with $\beta_1 = 0$.

---

### Equivalence of $F$ Test and $t$ Test

For a given $\alpha$ level, the $F$ test of $\beta_1 = 0$ versus $\beta_1 \neq 0$ is **equivalent algebraically** to the two-tailed $t$ test. To see this, recall from (2.51) that:

$$SSR = b_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$$

Thus, we can write:

$$F^* = \frac{SSR \div 1}{SSE \div (n-2)} = \frac{b_1^2 \sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2}{MSE}$$

But since $s^2\{b_1\} = MSE/\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$, we obtain:

$$F^* = \frac{b_1^2}{s^2\{b_1\}} = \left(\frac{b_1}{s\{b_1\}}\right)^2 = (t^*)^2$$ (2.63)

The last step follows because the $t^*$ statistic for testing whether or not $\beta_1 = 0$ is by (2.17):

$$t^* = \frac{b_1}{s\{b_1\}}$$

In the Toluca Company example, we calculated earlier that $F^* = 105.9$. From earlier work, we have $t^* = 10.29$ (see Figure 2.2). Thus, we see that $(10.29)^2 = 105.9$.

**Corresponding to the relation between $t^*$ and $F^*$**, we have the following relation between the **required percentiles** of the $t$ and $F$ distributions for the tests: $[t(1-\alpha/2; n-2)]^2 = F(1-\alpha; 1, n-2)$.

In our tests on $\beta_1$, these percentiles were $[t(0.975; 23)]^2 = (2.069)^2 = 4.28 = F(0.95; 1, 23)$. Remember that the $t$ test is two-tailed whereas the $F$ test is one-tailed.

**Thus, at any given $\alpha$ level**, we can use either the $t$ test or the $F$ test for testing $\beta_1 = 0$ versus $\beta_1 \neq 0$. Whenever one test leads to $H_0$, so will the other, and correspondingly for $H_a$.

> **The $t$ test, however, is more flexible** since it can be used for one-sided alternatives involving $\beta_1(\leq \geq) 0$ versus $\beta_1(> <) 0$, while the $F$ test cannot.

## 2.9 Descriptive Measures of Linear Association between $X$ and $Y$

### Why Descriptive Measures Are Useful

We've discussed the major uses of regression analysis:
- **Estimation** of parameters and mean responses
- **Prediction** of new observations

...without mentioning the **"degree of linear association"** between $X$ and $Y$, or similar terms.

**The reason**: The usefulness of estimates or predictions depends upon:
- The **width** of the interval
- The **user's needs for precision**

which can vary from one application to another.

Hence, **no single descriptive measure** of the "degree of linear association" can capture the essential information as to whether a given regression relation is useful in any particular application.

---

### When Are Descriptive Measures Useful?

Nevertheless, there are times when the **degree of linear association** is of interest in its own right. We now briefly discuss **two descriptive measures** that are frequently used in practice to describe the degree of linear association between $X$ and $Y$.

---

## Coefficient of Determination

### Understanding $SSTO$ and $SSE$

We saw earlier that $SSTO$ measures the variation in the observations $Y_i$, or the **uncertainty in predicting $Y$** when no account of the predictor variable $X$ is taken. Thus, $SSTO$ is a measure of the **uncertainty in predicting $Y$** when $X$ is **not considered**.

Similarly, $SSE$ measures the variation in the $Y_i$ when a regression model utilizing the predictor variable $X$ is employed. A natural measure of the **effect** of $X$ in reducing the variation in $Y$, i.e., in reducing the uncertainty in predicting $Y$, is to express the **reduction in variation** ($SSTO - SSE = SSR$) as a **proportion** of the total variation:

$$R^2 = \frac{SSR}{SSTO} = 1 - \frac{SSE}{SSTO}$$ (2.72)

The measure $R^2$ is called the **coefficient of determination**.

Since $0 \leq SSE \leq SSTO$, it follows that:

$$0 \leq R^2 \leq 1$$ (2.72a)

---

### Interpreting $R^2$

We may interpret $R^2$ as the **proportionate reduction of total variation** associated with the use of the predictor variable $X$. Thus, the larger $R^2$ is, the more the total variation of $Y$ is reduced by introducing the predictor variable $X$.

**The limiting values of $R^2$ occur as follows:**

**1. When all observations fall on the fitted regression line**, then $SSE = 0$ and $R^2 = 1$.

This case is shown in Figure 2.8a. Here, **the predictor variable $X$ accounts for all variation** in the observations $Y_i$.

**2. When the fitted regression line is horizontal** so that $b_1 = 0$ and $\hat{Y}_i \equiv \bar{Y}$, then $SSE = SSTO$ and $R^2 = 0$.

This case is shown in Figure 2.8b. Here, there is **no linear association** between $X$ and $Y$ in the sample data, and **the predictor variable $X$ is of no help in reducing the variation** in the observations $Y_i$ with linear regression.

![Scatter plots when R² = 1 and R² = 0](placeholder-r-squared-extremes.png)

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company ($R^2$)

For the Toluca Company example, we obtained $SSTO = 307,203$ and $SSR = 252,378$. Hence:

$$R^2 = \frac{252,378}{307,203} = 0.822$$

Thus, the **variation in work hours is reduced by 82.2%** when lot size is considered.

**The MINITAB output** in Figure 2.2 shows the coefficient of determination $R^2$ labeled as **R-sq in percent form**. The output also shows the coefficient **R-sq(adj)**, which will be explained in Chapter 6.

---

### Limitations of $R^2$

We noted that **no single measure will be adequate** for describing the usefulness of a regression model for different applications. Unfortunately, $R^2$ is subject to **serious misunderstandings**. We consider now three common misunderstandings:

---

#### Misunderstanding 1: High $R^2$ Means Useful Predictions

**A high coefficient of determination indicates that useful predictions can be made.**

**This is not necessarily correct.**

In the Toluca Company example, we saw that the coefficient of determination was high ($R^2 = 0.82$). Yet the 90% prediction interval for the next lot, consisting of 100 units, was **wide** (332 to 507 hours) and not precise enough to permit management to schedule workers effectively.

---

#### Misunderstanding 2: High $R^2$ Means Good Fit

**A high coefficient of determination indicates that the estimated regression line is a good fit.**

**Again, this is not necessarily correct.**

Figure 2.9a shows a scatter plot where the coefficient of determination is high ($R^2 = 0.69$). Yet a linear regression function would **not be a good fit** since the regression relation is curvilinear.

![High R² but poor fit due to curvature](placeholder-r-squared-misunderstanding-2.png)

---

#### Misunderstanding 3: Low $R^2$ Means No Relationship

**A coefficient of determination near zero indicates that $X$ and $Y$ are not related.**

**This also is not necessarily correct.**

Figure 2.9b shows a scatter plot where the coefficient of determination between $X$ and $Y$ is $R^2 = 0.02$. Yet $X$ and $Y$ are **strongly related**; however, the relationship between the two variables is **curvilinear**.

![Low R² but strong curvilinear relationship](placeholder-r-squared-misunderstanding-3.png)

---

**Misunderstanding 1** arises because $R^2$ measures **only a relative reduction** from $SSTO$ and provides **no information about absolute precision** for estimating a mean response or predicting a new observation.

**Misunderstandings 2 and 3** arise because $R^2$ measures the degree of **linear** association between $X$ and $Y$, whereas the **actual regression relation** may be curvilinear.

---

## Coefficient of Correlation

### Definition

A measure of **linear association** between $Y$ and $X$ when **both $Y$ and $X$ are random** is the **coefficient of correlation**. This measure is the **signed square root** of $R^2$:

$$r = \pm\sqrt{R^2}$$ (2.73)

A **plus or minus sign** is attached to this measure according to whether the slope of the fitted regression line is **positive or negative**. Thus, the range of $r$ is: $-1 \leq r \leq 1$.

---

### 📊 Example: Toluca Company (Correlation Coefficient)

For the Toluca Company example, we obtained $R^2 = 0.822$. Treating $X$ as a random variable, the correlation coefficient here is:

$$r = +\sqrt{0.822} = 0.907$$

The **plus sign** is affixed since $b_1$ is positive. We take up the topic of correlation analysis in more detail in Section 2.11.

---

### Comments on $R^2$ and Correlation

**1. The value taken by $R^2$ in a given sample tends to be affected by the spacing of the $X$ observations.**

This is implied in (2.72). $SSE$ is not affected systematically by the spacing of the $X_i$ since, for regression model (2.1), $\sigma^2\{Y_i\} = \sigma^2$ at all $X$ levels. However, the wider the spacing of the $X_i$ in the sample is, the **greater will tend to be the spread** in the $Y_i$ observations and hence the greater $SSTO$ will be. Consequently, the wider the $X_i$ are spaced, the **higher $R^2$ will tend to be**.

**2. The regression sum of squares $SSR$ is often called the "explained variation" in $Y$, and the residual sum of squares $SSE$ is called the "unexplained variation."**

The coefficient $R^2$ then is interpreted in terms of the **proportion of the total variation** in $Y$ ($SSTO$) which has been "explained" by $X$. Unfortunately, this terminology frequently is taken literally, and hence, misunderstood.

> **Remember**: In a regression model there is **no implication** that $Y$ necessarily depends on $X$ in a **causal or explanatory** sense. With nonexperimental data, both the $X$ and $Y$ variables may be simultaneously influenced by other variables not in the regression model. On the other hand, the existence of a regression relation in controlled experiments is often good evidence of a cause-and-effect relation.

**3. Regression models do not contain a parameter to be estimated by $R^2$ or $r$.**

These are simply **descriptive measures** of the degree of linear association between $X$ and $Y$ in the sample observations that may, or may not, be useful in any instance.

---

## 2.10 Considerations in Applying Regression Analysis

We've now discussed the major uses of regression analysis:
- To make inferences about the regression parameters
- To estimate the mean response for a given $X$
- To predict a new observation $Y$ for a given $X$

It remains to make a **few cautionary remarks** about implementing applications of regression analysis.

---

### 1. Regression for Prediction/Forecasting

**Frequently, regression analysis is used to make inferences for the future.**

For instance:
- For **planning staffing** requirements, a school board may wish to predict future enrollments by using a regression model containing several demographic variables as predictor variables
- In applications of this type, it's important to remember that the **validity of the regression application** depends upon whether **basic causal conditions** in the period ahead will be similar to those in existence during the period upon which the regression analysis is based

This caution applies whether:
- Mean responses are to be estimated
- New observations predicted
- Regression parameters estimated

---

### 2. Predicting the Predictor Variable

**In predicting new observations on $Y$, the predictor variable $X$ itself often has to be predicted.**

For instance, we mentioned earlier the **prediction of company sales** for next year from the demographic projection of the number of persons 16 years of age or older next year.

**A prediction of company sales** under these circumstances is a **conditional prediction**, dependent upon the correctness of the population projection. It's easy to forget the conditional nature of this type of prediction.

---

### 3. Extrapolation Beyond the Range of Observations

**Another caution deals with inferences pertaining to levels of the predictor variable that fall outside the range of observations.**

Unfortunately, this situation frequently occurs in practice. **A company** that predicts its sales from a regression relation of company sales to disposable personal income will often find the **level of disposable personal income** of interest (e.g., for the year ahead) to fall **beyond the range of past data**.

**If the $X$ level does not fall far beyond this range**, one may have **reasonable confidence** in the application of the regression analysis.

**On the other hand, if the $X$ level falls far beyond the range of past data**, **extreme caution** should be exercised since one **cannot be sure** that the regression function that fits the past data is appropriate over the wider range of the predictor variable.

---

### 4. No Cause-and-Effect Conclusions from Observational Data

**A statistical test that leads to the conclusion that $\beta_1 \neq 0$ does not establish a cause-and-effect relation between the predictor and response variables.**

As we noted in Chapter 1, with **nonexperimental data** both the $X$ and $Y$ variables may be simultaneously influenced by **other variables not in the regression model**.

**On the other hand**, the existence of a regression relation in **controlled experiments** is often **good evidence** of a cause-and-effect relation.

---

### 5. Multiple Inferences from the Same Data

**We should note again that frequently we wish to estimate several mean responses or predict several new observations for different levels of the predictor variable**, and that **special problems arise** in this case.

The **confidence coefficients** for the limits (2.33) for estimating a mean response and for the prediction limits (2.36) for a new observation apply only for a **single level** $X$ for a given sample.

**In Chapter 4**, we discuss how to make several inferences from a given sample.

---

### 6. Measurement Errors in Predictor Variable

**Finally, when observations on the predictor variable $X$ are subject to measurement errors**, the resulting **parameter estimates are generally no longer unbiased**.

**In Chapter 4**, we discuss several ways to handle this situation.

---

## 2.11 Normal Correlation Models

### Distinction Between Regression and Correlation Model

The **normal error regression model (2.1)**, which has been used throughout this chapter and which will continue to be used, assumes that the $X$ values are **known constants**.

As a consequence of this, **the confidence coefficients and risks of errors** refer to **repeated sampling** when the $X$ values are kept the same from sample to sample.

---

### When is a Correlation Model Appropriate?

**Frequently, it may not be appropriate to consider the $X$ values as known constants.**

For instance:
- Consider **regressing daily bathing suit sales** by a department store on **mean daily temperature**. Surely, the department store cannot control daily temperatures, so it would not be meaningful to think of **repeated sampling where temperature levels are the same** from sample to sample.

- As a second example, an analyst may use a **correlation model** for the two variables "height of person" and "weight of person" in a study of a sample of persons, each variable being taken as random. The analyst might wish to study the **relation between the two variables** or might be interested in making inferences about weight on the basis of the person's height, in making inferences about height on the basis of weight, or in both.

---

### Other Examples for Correlation Models

Other examples where a **correlation model**, rather than a regression model, may be appropriate are:

1. To study the **relation between service station sales of gasoline** and **sales of auxiliary products**.

2. To study the **relation between company net income** determined by generally accepted accounting principles and **net income according to tax regulations**.

3. To study the **relation between blood pressure** and **age in human subjects**.

---

### The Most Widely Employed Correlation Model

The **correlation model** most widely employed is the **normal correlation model**. We discuss it here for the case of **two variables**.

---

## Bivariate Normal Distribution

The **normal correlation model** for the case of two variables is based on the **bivariate normal distribution**.

Let us denote the two variables as $Y_1$ and $Y_2$. (We do not use the notation $X$ and $Y$ here because both variables play a **symmetrical role** in correlation analysis.)

We say that $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are **jointly normally distributed** if the density function of their **joint distribution** is that of the bivariate normal distribution.

---

### Density Function

**Figure 2.10** contains a SYSTAT three-dimensional plot of a bivariate normal probability distribution.

![Bivariate normal distribution 3D surface](placeholder-bivariate-normal-3d.png)

The probability distribution is a **surface in three-dimensional space**. For every pair of $(Y_1, Y_2)$ values, the density $f(Y_1, Y_2)$ represents the **height of the surface** at that point. The surface is continuous, and **probability corresponds to volume under the surface**.

The **density function** of the bivariate normal distribution is:

$$f(Y_1, Y_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_1\sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho_{12}^2}} \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho_{12}^2)}\left[\left(\frac{Y_1 - \mu_1}{\sigma_1}\right)^2 - 2\rho_{12}\left(\frac{Y_1 - \mu_1}{\sigma_1}\right)\left(\frac{Y_2 - \mu_2}{\sigma_2}\right) + \left(\frac{Y_2 - \mu_2}{\sigma_2}\right)^2\right]\right\}$$ (2.74)

Note that this density function involves **five parameters**: $\mu_1, \mu_2, \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \rho_{12}$. We explain the meaning of these parameters shortly. First, let us consider a **graphic representation** of the bivariate normal distribution.

---

### Marginal Distributions

If $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are **jointly normally distributed**, it can be shown that their **marginal distributions** have the following characteristics:

**The marginal distribution of $Y_1$ is normal with mean $\mu_1$ and standard deviation $\sigma_1$:**

$$f_1(Y_1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_1} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_1 - \mu_1}{\sigma_1}\right)^2\right]$$ (2.75a)

**The marginal distribution of $Y_2$ is normal with mean $\mu_2$ and standard deviation $\sigma_2$:**

$$f_2(Y_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_2} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_2 - \mu_2}{\sigma_2}\right)^2\right]$$ (2.75b)

Thus, when $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are **jointly normally distributed**, each of the two variables by itself is **normally distributed**.

> **The converse, however, is not generally true**: If $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are each normally distributed, they need not be jointly normally distributed in accord with (2.74).

---

### Meaning of Parameters

The **five parameters** of the bivariate normal density function (2.74) have the following meaning:

1. **$\mu_1$ and $\sigma_1$** are the **mean and standard deviation** of the marginal distribution of $Y_1$.

2. **$\mu_2$ and $\sigma_2$** are the **mean and standard deviation** of the marginal distribution of $Y_2$.

3. **$\rho_{12}$** is the **coefficient of correlation** between the random variables $Y_1$ and $Y_2$. This coefficient is denoted by $\rho[Y_1, Y_2]$ in Appendix A, using the correlation operator notation, and defined in (A.25a):

$$\rho_{12} = \rho[Y_1, Y_2] = \frac{\sigma_{12}}{\sigma_1\sigma_2}$$ (2.76)

Here, $\sigma_1$ and $\sigma_2$, as just mentioned, denote the **standard deviations** of $Y_1$ and $Y_2$, and $\sigma_{12}$ denotes the **covariance** $\sigma[Y_1, Y_2]$ between $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ as defined in (A.21):

$$\sigma_{12} = \sigma[Y_1, Y_2] = E\{(Y_1 - \mu_1)(Y_2 - \mu_2)\}$$ (2.77)

Note that $\sigma_{12} \equiv \sigma_{21}$ and $\rho_{12} \equiv \rho_{21}$.

---

### Properties of the Correlation Coefficient

**If $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are independent**, $\sigma_{12} = 0$ according to (A.28) so that $\rho_{12} = 0$.

**If $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are positively related**—that is, $Y_1$ tends to be large when $Y_2$ is large, or small when $Y_2$ is small—$\sigma_{12}$ is positive and so is $\rho_{12}$.

**On the other hand, if $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are negatively related**—that is, $Y_1$ tends to be large when $Y_2$ is small, or vice versa—$\sigma_{12}$ is negative and so is $\rho_{12}$.

**The coefficient of correlation $\rho_{12}$ can take on any value between $-1$ and $1$ inclusive.** It assumes $1$ if the linear relation between $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ is **perfectly positive** (direct) and $-1$ if it is **perfectly negative** (inverse).

---

## Conditional Inferences

### Why Conditional Probability Distributions?

As noted, **one principal use of a bivariate correlation model** is to make **conditional inferences** regarding one variable, given the other variable. Suppose $Y_1$ represents a **service station's gasoline sales** and $Y_2$ its **sales of auxiliary products**. We may then wish to predict a service station's sales of auxiliary products $Y_2$, given that its gasoline sales are $Y_1 = \$5,500$.

**Such conditional inferences** require the use of **conditional probability distributions**, which we discuss next.

---

### Conditional Probability Distribution of $Y_1$

The **density function** of the conditional probability distribution of $Y_1$ for any given value of $Y_2$ is denoted by $f(Y_1|Y_2)$ and defined as follows:

$$f(Y_1|Y_2) = \frac{f(Y_1, Y_2)}{f_2(Y_2)}$$ (2.78)

where $f(Y_1, Y_2)$ is the **joint density function** of $Y_1$ and $Y_2$, and $f_2(Y_2)$ is the **marginal density function** of $Y_2$.

When $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ are **jointly normally distributed** according to (2.74) so that the marginal density function $f_2(Y_2)$ is given by (2.75b), it can be shown that:

**The conditional probability distribution of $Y_1$ for any given value of $Y_2$ is normal with mean $\alpha_{1|2} + \beta_{12}Y_2$ and standard deviation $\sigma_{1|2}$ and its density function is:**

$$f(Y_1|Y_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_{1|2}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_1 - \alpha_{1|2} - \beta_{12}Y_2}{\sigma_{1|2}}\right)^2\right]$$ (2.79)

---

### Parameters of the Conditional Distribution

The parameters $\alpha_{1|2}$, $\beta_{12}$, and $\sigma_{1|2}$ of the **conditional probability distributions of $Y_1$** are functions of the parameters of the **joint probability distribution** (2.74), as follows:

$$\alpha_{1|2} = \mu_1 - \mu_2\rho_{12}\frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_2}$$ (2.80a)

$$\beta_{12} = \rho_{12}\frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_2}$$ (2.80b)

$$\sigma_{1|2}^2 = \sigma_1^2(1 - \rho_{12}^2)$$ (2.80c)

**The parameter $\alpha_{1|2}$** is the **intercept** of the line of regression of $Y_1$ on $Y_2$, and **the parameter $\beta_{12}$** is the **slope** of this line. Thus we find that the **conditional distribution of $Y_1$, given $Y_2$**, is equivalent to the **normal error regression model (1.24)**.

---

### Conditional Probability Distributions of $Y_2$

The **random variables** $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ **play symmetrical roles** in the bivariate normal probability distribution (2.74). Hence, it follows:

**The conditional probability distribution of $Y_2$ for any given value of $Y_1$ is normal with mean $\alpha_{2|1} + \beta_{21}Y_1$ and standard deviation $\sigma_{2|1}$ and its density function is:**

$$f(Y_2|Y_1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_{2|1}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Y_2 - \alpha_{2|1} - \beta_{21}Y_1}{\sigma_{2|1}}\right)^2\right]$$ (2.81)

The parameters $\alpha_{2|1}$, $\beta_{21}$, and $\sigma_{2|1}$ of the conditional probability distributions of $Y_2$ are functions of the parameters of the joint probability distribution (2.74), as follows:

$$\alpha_{2|1} = \mu_2 - \mu_1\rho_{12}\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}$$ (2.82a)

$$\beta_{21} = \rho_{12}\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}$$ (2.82b)

$$\sigma_{2|1}^2 = \sigma_2^2(1 - \rho_{12}^2)$$ (2.82c)

---

### Important Characteristics of Conditional Distributions

**Three important characteristics** of the conditional probability distributions of $Y_1$ are **normality, linear regression, and constant variance**. We take up each of these in turn.

---

#### 1. Normality

**The conditional probability distribution of $Y_1$ for any given value of $Y_2$ is normal.**

Imagine that we **slice a bivariate normal distribution vertically** at a given value of $Y_2$, say, at $Y_{h2}$. That is, we slice it **parallel to the $Y_1$ axis**. This slicing is shown in Figure 2.11.

![Vertical slice of bivariate normal at Y₂ = Yₕ₂](placeholder-bivariate-slice-vertical.png)

The exposed cross section has the shape of a **normal distribution**, and after being scaled so that its area is 1, it portrays the **conditional probability distribution of $Y_1$, given that $Y_2 = Y_{h2}$**.

**This property of normality holds no matter what the value $Y_{h2}$ is.** Thus, whenever we slice the bivariate normal distribution **parallel to the $Y_1$ axis**, we obtain (after proper scaling) **a normal conditional probability distribution**.

---

#### 2. Linear Regression

**The means of the conditional probability distributions of $Y_1$ fall on a straight line, and hence are a linear function of $Y_2$:**

$$E[Y_1|Y_2] = \alpha_{1|2} + \beta_{12}Y_2$$ (2.83)

**2. The means of the conditional probability distributions of $Y_1$ fall on a straight line**, and hence are a **linear function** of $Y_2$:

$$E\{Y_1|Y_2\} = \alpha_{1|2} + \beta_{12}Y_2$$ (2.83)

Since the **slope** $\beta_{12}$ and the **intercept** $\alpha_{1|2}$ of the regression line are **parameters** of the conditional distributions, this line is sometimes called the **population regression line** or the **true regression line**.

---

#### 3. Constant Variance

**The variances of the conditional distributions** are all equal, irrespective of the level of $Y_2$. This follows from (2.80c), which shows that $\sigma_{1|2}^2$ does **not depend on the level** of $Y_2$.

> **These three characteristics** are exactly the same as those in the **normal error regression model (1.24)**, where $Y_1$ corresponds to $Y$ and $Y_2$ corresponds to $X$.

---

### Summary of Conditional Distributions

In summary:
- The **conditional probability distributions** of $Y_1$, given $Y_2$, correspond to the **normal error regression model (1.24)**.
- Similarly, the **conditional probability distributions** of $Y_2$, given $Y_1$, also correspond to the **normal error regression model**.

Thus, **all inferences we developed for the normal error regression model** apply to inferences based on the **conditional probability distributions** in the bivariate normal case.

---

## Final Summary: Normal Correlation vs. Regression Model

To summarize:
- The **normal correlation model (2.1)** has been our workhorse throughout this chapter
- It assumes the $X$ values are **known constants**
- **Confidence coefficients and risks** refer to **repeated sampling** where $X$ values stay the same

When both $X$ and $Y$ are **random** (e.g., height and weight of persons):
- Use the **bivariate normal correlation model**
- **Conditional inferences** about one variable given the other use the same methods as regression
- The difference is conceptual: both variables are random, not one fixed
