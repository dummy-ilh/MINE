# Chapter 1: The Problem BERT Solves

Let's build this up like a story — starting from what we *had*, what broke, and why BERT was the answer.

---

## 1.1 The Core Challenge: Language is Contextual

Read these two sentences:

> "I went to the **bank** to fish."
> "I went to the **bank** to deposit money."

You instantly know "bank" means something different in each. How? You used the **surrounding words** as context.

Now here's the brutal truth about classical NLP: **for decades, we couldn't do this.**

---

## 1.2 Era 1: Bag of Words (Before Neural Nets)

The oldest approach: represent a sentence as a *count of words*, ignoring order entirely.

**Example:**

```
"The cat sat on the mat"
→ {the: 2, cat: 1, sat: 1, on: 1, mat: 1}
```

**What's wrong?**
- "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog" produce the **identical** representation
- No meaning, no order, no context
- Works okay for simple topic classification, useless for anything deeper

---

## 1.3 Era 2: Word2Vec — A Huge Step Forward

Word2Vec (2013, also Google) was a breakthrough. It gave every word a **dense vector** learned from co-occurrence patterns in a large corpus.

**How it works (intuition):**
Words that appear in similar contexts get similar vectors. Trained on billions of sentences, it learns:

```
vector("king") - vector("man") + vector("woman") ≈ vector("queen")
```

This is genuinely remarkable. Arithmetic on meaning.

**But here's the fatal flaw:**

Every word gets **exactly one vector**, fixed forever, regardless of context.

```
"river bank"   → bank = [-0.2, 0.8, 0.1, ...]
"savings bank" → bank = [-0.2, 0.8, 0.1, ...]  ← SAME vector!
```

Word2Vec gives you the average meaning of a word across all its uses. That's not meaning. That's a blur.

**Other problems with Word2Vec:**
- Out-of-vocabulary words get no vector at all
- No sentence-level representation — just individual word vectors
- Can't handle "not good" vs "good" — negation is invisible

---

## 1.4 Era 3: RNNs and LSTMs — Adding Order

Recurrent Neural Networks process words **one at a time, left to right**, carrying a hidden state forward.

```
"The cat sat on the mat"

h0 → [The] → h1 → [cat] → h2 → [sat] → h3 → ...
```

Now word order matters! And the hidden state *h* carries memory of previous words.

**LSTMs** improved this with gating mechanisms to better control what gets remembered vs. forgotten.

**What got better:**
- Word order captured
- Some memory of past context
- Works reasonably well for short sequences

**What still breaks:**

**Problem 1 — One direction only.**
When encoding the word "bank" in position 3, the model has seen positions 0, 1, 2. It has NOT seen positions 4, 5, 6... yet those might be the very words that disambiguate "bank."

```
"I deposited money at the bank near the river"
                              ↑
         encoding happens here, "river" not seen yet
```

**Problem 2 — Long-range dependencies fail.**
By the time you reach word 50, the hidden state has been updated 50 times. Information from word 1 is *washed out*. LSTMs help but don't fully solve this.

**Problem 3 — Sequential = slow.**
You cannot process word 5 until you've finished word 4. No parallelism. Training on large corpora is painfully slow.

---

## 1.5 Era 4: The Transformer (2017) — Attention Is All You Need

The Transformer paper scrapped recurrence entirely. Instead of processing left-to-right, it looks at **all words simultaneously** and lets every word attend to every other word directly.

```
"I deposited money at the bank near the river"
         ↕         ↕    ↕    ↕    ↕    ↕    ↕
every word can directly "talk to" every other word
```

This solved the long-range dependency problem and enabled massive parallelism.

But the original Transformer was designed for **translation** — it has an encoder AND a decoder. The decoder is unidirectional (it can't look ahead, because it's generating words one at a time).

---

## 1.6 Era 4.5: GPT — Transformer for Language Modeling

GPT (OpenAI, 2018) took just the **decoder** part of the Transformer. It predicts the next word given all previous words.

```
"The cat sat on the ___"  → predicts "mat"
```

GPT is **unidirectional** by design — it must be, because during generation you don't know future words.

**For generation tasks: perfect.**
**For understanding tasks: you're leaving half the context on the table.**

When classifying sentiment or extracting entities, you don't need to generate anything. You just need to *understand*. And understanding benefits from seeing the full sentence at once.

---

## 1.7 The BERT Insight

BERT (Google, October 2018) asked: *what if we take just the encoder side of the Transformer and pre-train it bidirectionally?*

Instead of predicting the next word, BERT randomly **masks** words and predicts them using context from **both directions** simultaneously.

```
"The [MASK] sat on the mat"
         ↑
  uses "The" AND "sat on the mat" to predict "cat"
```

This forces the model to build rich, bidirectional, contextual representations.

**The result:**

```
"river bank"   → bank = [0.3, -0.1, 0.8, ...]   ← pulled toward "river", "water", "nature"
"savings bank" → bank = [-0.5, 0.6, 0.2, ...]   ← pulled toward "money", "deposit", "finance"
```

Same word. Completely different vectors. That's contextual embedding.

---

## 1.8 The Complete Timeline at a Glance

```
Bag of Words    → No order, no meaning, just counts
     ↓
Word2Vec        → Meaning via vectors, but one vector per word (no context)
     ↓
RNN/LSTM        → Order matters, some memory, but left-to-right only + slow
     ↓
Transformer     → All positions simultaneously, but built for translation
     ↓
GPT             → Transformer for language, but unidirectional (generation only)
     ↓
BERT            → Bidirectional Transformer encoder, deep contextual understanding
```

---

## 1.9 What BERT Is, and What It Isn't

| | BERT |
|---|---|
| **Architecture** | Transformer encoder only |
| **Directionality** | Bidirectional (sees full context) |
| **Output** | Contextual vector per token |
| **Use case** | Understanding, classification, QA, NER |
| **NOT good for** | Text generation, translation |
| **How it's used** | Pre-trained on massive text → fine-tuned on your task |

---

## Chapter 1 Summary

The three problems BERT was built to solve:

1. **Static embeddings** (Word2Vec) — one vector per word, no context sensitivity
2. **Unidirectionality** (RNNs, GPT) — can't use right-side context when encoding
3. **Sequential processing** (RNNs) — can't parallelize, poor long-range memory

BERT's solution: a Transformer encoder that reads the entire sequence at once, pre-trained by predicting masked words using both left AND right context.
