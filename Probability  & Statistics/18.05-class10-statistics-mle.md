## 10a — Introduction to Statistics

### The Big Shift

Up to now, the course has been about probability: given a known distribution, compute probabilities. Statistics reverses the direction. We observe data and ask: *what can we infer about the distribution that generated it?*

This is the fundamental inversion at the heart of statistics:

$$\underbrace{\text{Probability}}_{\text{model} \to \text{data}} \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad \underbrace{\text{Statistics}}_{\text{data} \to \text{model}}$$

### Three Phases of Statistical Practice

**1. Experimental design** — before any data exists. This is often the most consequential phase. A poorly designed experiment produces data from which no valid inference is possible. R.A. Fisher put it sharply: consulting a statistician after an experiment is finished is like asking them to perform a post-mortem. The experiment has already died.

**2. Descriptive statistics** — summarising and visualising the raw data. Means, medians, interquartile ranges, histograms, scatterplots, empirical CDFs. The goal is to find structure: does the data look like it could follow a normal distribution? Are there outliers? Are there clusters?

**3. Inferential statistics** — drawing conclusions about the world from the data. This is where we specify a parametric model and use data to estimate its parameters. For example: assuming gestational length follows $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, estimate $\mu$ and $\sigma$ from 500 pregnancies. Or: modelling an election as Bernoulli($p$), estimate $p$ from polling data.

> Crucially, we can rarely make definitive statements — the data itself comes from a random process. Every inference comes with probability statements. Misunderstanding what those statements mean is a persistent source of real-world error.

### The Sally Clark Warning

In 1999 in the UK, Sally Clark was convicted of murdering her two infant children, a conviction based heavily on a catastrophic misuse of statistics — incorrectly ruling out natural causes. Her conviction was overturned in 2003, but she died in 2007 from the aftermath of her imprisonment.

This is not an abstract cautionary tale. Misinterpreting statistical statements has caused real harm. In 18.05, understanding the *meaning* of statistical outputs is treated as seriously as computing them.

### What Is a Statistic?

> **A statistic is anything that can be computed from the collected data.**

This is deliberately broad. All of the following are statistics from 1000 die rolls: the average, the number of 6s, the sum of squares minus the count of even rolls.

What is **not** a statistic: the probability of rolling a 6. That's a property of the die, not of the data. You can *estimate* it with a statistic ("fraction of rolls that were 6"), but the true probability itself lives in the model, not in the data.

**Three examples to make the boundary crisp:**

- "The average survival time of these 200 patients" → **statistic** (computed from data in hand)
- "The expected survival time under this new treatment" → **not a statistic** (a property of the world)
- "The proportion of 1000 surveyed Massachusetts residents who support a proposal" → **statistic**
- "The proportion of *all* Massachusetts residents who support it" → **not a statistic** (we didn't query everyone)

**Two types of statistics in 18.05:**

- *Point statistics* — a single number, e.g. sample mean $\bar{x}_n$, sample std $s_n$
- *Interval statistics* — an interval $[a, b]$, i.e. a pair of point statistics; often written $\bar{x} \pm s$

### Bayes' Theorem: The Engine of Inference

This is worth restating now, because it will structure everything for the rest of the course.

$$P(H \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(D)}$$

In the language of statistics:

$$P(\text{hypothesis true} \mid \text{data}) = \frac{P(\text{data} \mid \text{hypothesis true}) \cdot P(\text{hypothesis true})}{P(\text{data})}$$

What we *want* is on the left: the probability our hypothesis is true given what we've observed. The terms on the right involve the likelihood (how probable is this data if the hypothesis holds), the prior (our belief before seeing data), and the normalising constant.

When all three terms on the right are known exactly, we can compute the posterior exactly — this is what we'll do in the next few classes. When they're not known, we need other strategies.

**Example — Disease screening redux:**

A test has 1% false positive rate and 1% false negative rate. Disease prevalence is 0.002.

A person tests positive. What's the probability they actually have the disease?

$$P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease}) \cdot P(\text{disease})}{P(\text{positive})}$$

$$= \frac{0.99 \cdot 0.002}{0.99 \cdot 0.002 + 0.01 \cdot 0.998} = \frac{0.00198}{0.00198 + 0.00998} \approx 0.166$$

Before the test: 0.2% chance of disease. After a positive test: 16.6%. The test is highly accurate, but because the disease is rare, even a positive result leaves you more likely disease-free than not.

> This is the base rate fallacy in action. A test's accuracy and a diagnosis's reliability are not the same thing. The prevalence of the disease matters enormously.

## 10b — Maximum Likelihood Estimation

### The Question MLE Answers

We have a parametric family of distributions — say, Binomial($n$, $p$) — and we've observed data. The parameter $p$ is unknown. 

MLE asks: **for which value of the parameter does the observed data have the highest probability?**

That value is the **maximum likelihood estimate (MLE)**, written $\hat{p}$.

The MLE is a point estimate — it gives a single number for the unknown parameter. Its defining property is that it is the parameter value that makes the observed data most probable.

### Terminology (set up carefully once, used everywhere)

Setting up notation cleanly before computing is essential in MLE problems. Here's the standard vocabulary:

| Term | Meaning | Example (coin flips) |
|---|---|---|
| **Experiment** | The random process | Flip coin 100 times |
| **Data** | The observed result | 55 heads |
| **Parameter** | Unknown quantity of interest | $p$ = P(heads) |
| **Likelihood** | $P(\text{data} \mid \text{parameter})$ as a function of the parameter | $P(55 \text{ heads} \mid p)$ |
| **MLE** | Parameter value maximising the likelihood | $\hat{p}$ |

### MLE for a Discrete Distribution — Coin Flips

**Setup:** Coin flipped 100 times, 55 heads. Find the MLE for $p$.

The likelihood — the probability of seeing exactly 55 heads as a function of $p$ — is:

$$L(p) = P(\text{55 heads} \mid p) = \binom{100}{55} p^{55}(1-p)^{45}$$

We want the $p$ that maximises this. Taking the derivative and setting it to zero:

$$\frac{d}{dp} L(p) = 0$$

After working through the algebra (easier via log likelihood — see Section 4), the answer is:

$$\hat{p} = \frac{55}{100} = 0.55$$

The MLE for $p$ is just the observed proportion of heads. This confirms the intuition: the most likely parameter is the one that matches what you actually saw.

> **Three important notes:**
> 1. The MLE is the fraction of heads in the data — the simplest possible summary
> 2. The MLE is a statistic — it is computed from data
> 3. Officially, you must verify the critical point is a maximum, not a minimum or saddle point (check the second derivative or boundary behaviour)

### The Likelihood is $P(\text{data} \mid \theta)$ — not $P(\theta \mid \text{data})$

This confusion is extremely common and must be stamped out early.

- **Likelihood:** $L(\theta) = P(\text{data} \mid \theta)$ — how probable is this data, *if* $\theta$ is true?
- **Posterior:** $P(\theta \mid \text{data})$ — how probable is $\theta$, *given* the data?

These are related by Bayes' theorem but are completely different quantities. The likelihood does **not** tell you the probability of the parameter. It tells you the plausibility of the data under each possible parameter value. We then find the parameter that makes the data most plausible.

### Multiple Data Points — Likelihood is a Product

When data consists of $n$ independent observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, independence means probabilities multiply:

$$L(\theta) = P(x_1, \ldots, x_n \mid \theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(x_i \mid \theta)$$

For a binomial family where $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ iid:

$$L(p) = \prod_{i=1}^n p^{x_i}(1-p)^{1-x_i} = p^{\sum x_i}(1-p)^{n - \sum x_i}$$

The MLE is again $\hat{p} = \bar{x}$ — the sample proportion.

## MLE for Continuous Distributions

### The Subtlety: Density, Not Probability

For continuous random variables, any exact value has probability zero — so $P(X = x \mid \theta) = 0$ for all $x$. We can't use probabilities directly.

Instead, we use the fact that for small ranges:

$$P(X \in [x, x + dx] \mid \theta) \approx f(x \mid \theta)\,dx$$

For $n$ independent continuous observations, the joint "probability" is approximately:

$$\prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i \mid \theta)\,dx_i$$

Since we're maximising over $\theta$ and the $dx_i$ factors are constants that don't depend on $\theta$, they drop out. We define the likelihood for continuous data as:

$$\boxed{L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i \mid \theta)}$$

This is the density evaluated at the data, treated as a function of $\theta$.

### Example — Exponential Lifetimes

**Setup:** Light bulb lifetimes follow $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ with unknown $\lambda$. Five bulbs have lifetimes $x_1 = 2, x_2 = 3, x_3 = 1, x_4 = 3, x_5 = 4$.

The pdf for each observation is $f(x \mid \lambda) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$. By independence:

$$L(\lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^5 \lambda e^{-\lambda x_i} = \lambda^5 e^{-\lambda(2+3+1+3+4)} = \lambda^5 e^{-13\lambda}$$

Taking log likelihood (see next section):

$$\ell(\lambda) = 5\ln\lambda - 13\lambda$$

$$\frac{d\ell}{d\lambda} = \frac{5}{\lambda} - 13 = 0 \implies \hat{\lambda} = \frac{5}{13}$$

The MLE is $\hat{\lambda} = \frac{n}{\sum x_i} = \frac{1}{\bar{x}}$ — the reciprocal of the sample mean. Since the mean of $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ is $1/\lambda$, this makes perfect intuitive sense: estimate the rate as the inverse of the observed average lifetime.

### Example — Normal Distribution

Data $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ iid from $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, both parameters unknown.

$$L(\mu, \sigma) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x_i - \mu)^2/2\sigma^2}$$

Taking log likelihood and finding partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} = 0 \implies \hat{\mu} = \bar{x}$$

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma} = 0 \implies \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2$$

The MLE for the mean is the sample mean. The MLE for the variance is the average squared deviation — note this uses $1/n$, not $1/(n-1)$ (the latter is the *unbiased* estimator, but not the MLE).

### Example — Uniform Distribution (No Calculus)

Data $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ iid from $U(0, \theta)$ with unknown $\theta$.

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\theta} = \frac{1}{\theta^n} \quad \text{for } \theta \geq \max(x_i)$$

This is strictly decreasing in $\theta$. To maximise it, make $\theta$ as small as possible — but $\theta$ must be at least as large as every observed value. Therefore:

$$\hat{\theta} = \max(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$$

No calculus needed. The MLE is the largest observed value. Notice that this estimator is guaranteed to underestimate $\theta$ — you can never observe a value larger than the true upper bound. This is a known bias of the uniform MLE.

## Log Likelihood — Why and How

### Why Take Logs?

The likelihood is a product of many terms, which is hard to differentiate. The log of a product is a sum, which is much easier.

$$\ell(\theta) = \ln L(\theta) = \ln \prod_i f(x_i \mid \theta) = \sum_i \ln f(x_i \mid \theta)$$

Since $\ln$ is strictly increasing, $L$ and $\ell$ are maximised at the same $\theta$. So maximising log likelihood is equivalent to maximising likelihood — but far more tractable.

### Example — Coin Flips via Log Likelihood

$$L(p) = \binom{100}{55} p^{55}(1-p)^{45}$$

$$\ell(p) = \ln\binom{100}{55} + 55\ln p + 45\ln(1-p)$$

$$\frac{d\ell}{dp} = \frac{55}{p} - \frac{45}{1-p} = 0$$

$$55(1-p) = 45p \implies 55 - 55p = 45p \implies \hat{p} = \frac{55}{100}$$

Verified: $\hat{p} = 0.55$. The binomial constant $\binom{100}{55}$ disappeared when we differentiated — it's a constant in $p$ and plays no role in the optimisation.

### The Invariance Property

The MLE has a clean behaviour under transformations: if $\hat{\theta}$ is the MLE for $\theta$, and $g$ is a one-to-one function, then $g(\hat{\theta})$ is the MLE for $g(\theta)$.

**Example:** If $\hat{\lambda} = 5/13$ is the MLE for the rate $\lambda$ of an exponential, then $1/\hat{\lambda} = 13/5$ is the MLE for the mean $1/\lambda$.

You don't need to re-derive the MLE for transformed parameters — just transform the MLE you already have.

## Key Traps and Gotchas

**1. Likelihood $\neq$ Posterior.** $L(\theta) = P(\text{data} \mid \theta)$ is not the probability that $\theta$ is true. These are related by Bayes' theorem — they're not the same thing.

**2. Always name the experiment, data, and parameter first.** Before computing anything, write out clearly: what is the experiment? what is the observed data? what is the parameter of interest? This prevents half the common errors.

**3. For continuous data, the likelihood is the joint density, not probability.** The $dx$ factors drop out when maximising, but the conceptual step matters: we switched from probability to density.

**4. Likelihood for independent data is a product.** Multi-observation likelihood = product of individual likelihoods. Forget this and you'll maximise the wrong thing.

**5. Log likelihood is almost always easier.** Default to it. The log turns products into sums, constants become additive and vanish under differentiation.

**6. Check it's actually a maximum.** A critical point of $\ell(\theta)$ could be a minimum. Either check the second derivative or use boundary behaviour (for most standard distributions the likelihood is 0 at the boundary, so interior critical points are maxima).

**7. MLE can be biased.** The uniform MLE $\hat{\theta} = \max(x_i)$ is a clear example — it always underestimates $\theta$. The normal MLE for variance ($1/n$ instead of $1/(n-1)$) is another. MLE maximises likelihood, not unbiasedness.

**8. The statistic/parameter distinction.** $\hat{p}$ is a statistic — it varies with the data. $p$ is a parameter — it's a fixed (unknown) property of the world. Don't conflate them.
